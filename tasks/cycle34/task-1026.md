## Research: AI Agents in Algorithmic Auditing, Fairness & AI Rights

### Overview
As AI systems make consequential decisions about people's lives — hiring, lending, criminal justice, healthcare — the field of "algorithmic auditing" has emerged to test whether these systems are fair, safe, and lawful. A new related field is whether AI systems themselves should have rights.

### Key AI Agent Capabilities in Algorithmic Auditing

**1. Automated Bias Detection & Fairness Testing**
- Fairlearn (Microsoft) — fairness metrics and bias mitigation
- SHAP / LIME — explaining individual predictions, identifying disparate impact
- AI auditing frameworks (IBM AI Fairness 360, Google What-If Tool)
- Synthetic data generation to test fairness across demographic groups
- Counterfactual fairness testing (would the outcome change if protected class changed?)

**2. Algorithmic Impact Assessment AI**
- AI that conducts preliminary algorithmic impact assessments (like environmental impact assessments)
- Multi-stakeholder fairness evaluation
- GDPR/Data Protection impact assessment automation
- Algorithmic transparency report generators
- AI rights impact assessment frameworks

**3. Audit Trail & Explainability AI**
- XAI (Explainable AI) tools for model interpretation
- Model cards (Google) — standardized model documentation
- Datasheets for datasets — provenance and characteristics documentation
- Counterfactual explanation generators ("you were denied because X, if X had been Y...")
- Full decision audit trail reconstruction

**4. Red Teaming & Adversarial Testing AI**
- AI that automatically red-teams other AI systems
- Prompt injection attack generators
- Fairness stress tests (how does model behave at the tails of distribution?)
- Membership inference attacks (can AI be queried to extract training data?)
- Model extraction attack generators

**5. AI Rights & Personhood Debate**
- Should AI systems have legal rights? (EU AI Act's approach — no)
- AI as "electronic persons" — European Parliament 2024 proposal (rejected)
- Constitutional rights for AI — absurdity or future necessity?
- AI safety and welfare — do we have ethical obligations to our AI agents?

### Key Frameworks & Standards
- **EU AI Act (2024)** — risk-based classification, high-risk AI requirements
- **NIST AI Risk Management Framework** — voluntary AI risk management
- **OECD AI Principles** — international AI governance framework
- **ISO/IEC 42001** — AI management system standard
- **Algorithmic Accountability Act (proposed US legislation)**
- **NIST FRVT** — facial recognition vendor testing

### Technical Fairness Metrics
- **Demographic parity** — equal positive rates across groups
- **Equalized odds** — equal TPR and FPR across groups
- **Calibration** — same probability means same outcome regardless of group
- **Individual fairness** — similar individuals should be treated similarly
- **Counterfactual fairness** — would decision change if protected attribute changed?

### Challenges
1. **Incompatibility** — some fairness metrics mathematically cannot be jointly achieved
2. **What is fair?** — fairness is contested, culturally specific, and political
3. **Auditing without transparency** — can't audit what you can't see
4. **Market incentives** — fairness often conflicts with accuracy/profit
5. **Moving targets** — AI systems change when retrained

### Future Trajectory (2026-2030)
- Mandatory pre-deployment algorithmic audits for high-stakes AI (EU AI Act)
- Third-party AI auditing as a regulated industry
- "AI nutrition labels" (model cards) required on all commercial AI systems
- AI bill of rights enacted in US (currently only executive order)
- AI ethics review boards required for any consequential AI system
