## Executive Summary

AI governance is the defining policy challenge of the next decade. Governments worldwide are racing to regulate AI without stifling innovation, while managing risks ranging from algorithmic discrimination to existential catastrophe. The EU AI Act (fully applicable 2026) is the world's first comprehensive AI law. The US has taken a more sector-specific, voluntary approach. China has implemented both restrictions and mandates. The challenge is that AI development is fundamentally transnational and moving faster than any single government's ability to understand it.

---

## Major Regulatory Frameworks

### EU AI Act (2024)
- **Risk-based approach**: Four tiers of AI risk (unacceptable, high, limited, minimal)
- **Unacceptable risk (banned)**: Social scoring by governments, real-time biometric surveillance in public (with exceptions), AI that manipulates vulnerable groups
- **High-risk AI**: Requires conformity assessment, documentation, human oversight, transparency (medical devices, hiring, credit, education)
- **GPAI (General Purpose AI)**: Large foundation models face additional obligations (summaries of training data, copyright compliance, systemic risk evaluations)
- **Fines**: Up to 7% of global revenue for violations
- **Timeline**: Fully applicable 2026; high-risk provisions apply from 2025

### US Approach (Sector-Specific)
- **Executive Order 14110 (2023)**: Safe, secure, trustworthy AI — directing agencies to issue guidance
- **NIST AI Risk Management Framework**: Voluntary but widely adopted
- **FCC, FTC, CFPB, FDA**: Sector-specific AI guidance from regulators
- **State laws**: California, Colorado, Illinois AI laws for specific domains (hiring, insurance, children)
- **Congress**: No comprehensive AI law yet; likely 2026-2027

### China
- **Generative AI regulations (2023)**: Content control, algorithmic recommendation rules, deep synthesis rules
- **Algorithmic recommendation rules**: Users can opt out of personalized recommendations
- **Generative AI Provisions**: Content must be "socialistic core values," no undermining state power
- **AI-generated content labeling**: Mandatory watermarking for AI-generated images and video

### UK
- **Pro-innovation approach**: Avoiding EU-style prescriptive regulation
- **AI Safety Institute**: Government body focused on frontier AI safety (mirrors US AISI)
- **Sector regulators**: Financial Conduct Authority, MHRA, ICO issuing sector guidance

## Key Governance Challenges

### Explainability vs. Performance
- The best AI models (large neural networks) are inherently less explainable
- Regulators demand explainability; developers demand performance — tension is real
- **Solution pathways**: Post-hoc explainability, interpretable-by-design models, hybrid approaches

### Accountability
- Who is responsible when AI causes harm? The developer? The deployer? The user?
- **Product liability**: Existing frameworks apply but may not fit AI's emergent behavior
- **Auditability**: Regulators need to be able to audit AI systems — which requires technical capacity governments currently lack

### Frontier AI Risk
- **Anthropic, OpenAI, DeepMind self-governance**: Internal safety evaluations, responsible scaling policies
- **AI Safety Institutes**: US and UK government bodies evaluating frontier models
- **Catastrophic risk**: AI that could pose existential threats — international coordination is nascent

### Copyright & Training Data
- **Ongoing lawsuits**: Getty v. Stability AI, NYT v. OpenAI — whether training on copyrighted data constitutes infringement
- **EU approach**: Training data transparency required; opt-out mechanisms for rights holders
- **Market solution**: Training data licenses becoming a business (Adobe Firefly trained on licensed data)

## International Coordination

### G7 Hiroshima Process (2023)
- Voluntary AI code of conduct for developers
- Focus on transparency, accountability, and safety

### Bletchley Declaration (2023)
- UK-hosted AI Safety Summit; 28 countries signed
- Acknowledged frontier AI risks requiring international cooperation

### UN AI Resolution (2024)
- First UN resolution on AI — focused on human rights, safety, and inclusive development
- Non-binding but symbolically significant

### OECD AI Principles
- 40+ countries signed principles on AI: inclusive growth, sustainable development, human-centered values, transparency, accountability

### The Problem: Enforcement
- No international body with authority to enforce AI rules
- Companies operate across borders; regulations stop at borders
- **The Brussels Effect**: EU regulations become de facto global standards because companies find it easier to comply globally than to build separate EU-specific products

## Technical Governance Tools

### AI Auditing
- **Red teaming**: Systematic adversarial testing of AI systems before deployment
- **Third-party auditors**: Companies like BABL AI, O'Neil Risk Consulting auditing algorithms for bias
- **Bug bounties**: Paying external hackers to find AI vulnerabilities

### Model Cards & Datasheets
- **Model Cards** (Mitchell et al.): Standardized documentation of model capabilities and limitations
- **Datasheets for Datasets**: Standard for documenting training data provenance
- **System cards**: Explaining how AI systems behave in practice

### Watermarking
- **AI-generated content watermarking**: Invisible signals in generated images, text, audio
- **Metadata standards**: C2PA (Coalition for Content Provenance and Authenticity) — adopted by Adobe, Microsoft, Google

## Frontier AI Governance

### xAI Risk
- **AI labs' own assessments**: Anthropic's Constitutional AI, OpenAI's Superalignment — internal safety work
- **Superalignment problem**: How to ensure AI systems remain aligned with human values as they become more capable
- **Remote oversight**: How to supervise AI systems that are too complex for humans to understand

### Compute Governance
- **Compute thresholds**: Reporting/declaring large training runs (like nuclear material accounting)
- **Chip export controls**: US restricting AI chip exports to China — a form of AI governance through hardware control
- **GPU tracking**: Monitoring who is training frontier models

---

## Bottom Line

AI governance is still in its formative years. The EU has drawn the first line; the US is finding its footing; China has its own approach; international coordination is nascent. The most important governance questions — how to handle frontier AI risk, how to ensure accountability for AI harm, how to coordinate across borders — are unresolved. The window for effective governance may be short: if AI capabilities continue to advance rapidly, regulatory frameworks may become obsolete before they're fully implemented.
