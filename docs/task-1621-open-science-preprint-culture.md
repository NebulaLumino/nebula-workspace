# Task 1621: Explore AI Agents in Open Science & Preprint Culture

## Overview

The open science movement and preprint culture have fundamentally reshaped how scientific knowledge is disseminated. Preprint servers like arXiv (physics, mathematics, computer science), bioRxiv (biology), medRxiv (health sciences), and ChemRxiv (chemistry) have dramatically reduced the time between discovery and publication, sometimes by months or even years. AI agents are now beginning to play a transformative role in this ecosystem, from automating quality checks to accelerating peer review and enhancing discoverability.

## How AI Agents Interact with Preprint Infrastructure

AI agents can be deployed as intelligent wrappers around preprint repositories. They can monitor new submissions in real time, extract structured metadata from unstructured preprint PDFs, and alert researchers to relevant new work before formal peer review has occurred. This is particularly valuable in fast-moving fields like machine learning, where arXiv receives thousands of new submissions weekly and keeping pace is nearly impossible for individual researchers.

More sophisticated agents can synthesize information across multiple preprints, identifying consensus positions, emerging debates, and research gaps. A researcher interested in a specific question could task an AI agent with: "Monitor all new preprints about transformer architectures for protein structure prediction and summarize weekly." The agent would continuously poll preprint APIs, filter by relevance, and deliver synthesized briefings.

## Automated Quality and Consistency Checks

Preprints, by definition, have not yet undergone peer review. This creates both opportunity and risk: ideas move faster, but so can errors. AI agents are being developed to perform automated quality screening—checking statistical soundness, identifying obvious methodological flaws, verifying that claimed results are consistent with the data shown, and flagging concerns before human reviewers see the work.

Tools like papISSTracker, SciA11y, and various language model-based checkers can scan preprints for accessibility issues, statistical reporting standards, and even potential p-hacking or HARKing indicators. While these tools don't replace human judgment, they act as a first-pass filter that improves the signal-to-noise ratio of the preprint ecosystem.

## Accelerating Peer Review with AI

The most contentious application of AI in preprint culture is AI-assisted or AI-led peer review. Some initiatives have experimented with using large language models to generate initial reviews of preprints, either to support human reviewers or, more controversially, as a standalone mechanism. The latter has faced significant backlash from the scientific community, with concerns about hallucination, lack of domain expertise, and the potential for gaming.

More defensible applications include AI agents that help reviewers by summarizing a preprint's contributions, identifying relevant prior work the authors may have missed, and suggesting specific questions to probe. These tools augment rather than replace human expertise.

## Discoverability and Semantic Search

One of the most immediate and broadly beneficial applications is using AI agents to enhance the discoverability of preprint knowledge. Traditional keyword-based search fails to capture the semantic content of preprints. AI-powered semantic search can understand that a researcher looking for "long-context attention mechanisms for genomic sequence analysis" might also be interested in work on sparse attention for DNA sequence modeling, even if the exact terminology differs.

Agents can also maintain personalized research feeds, tracking a researcher's interests and surfacing relevant preprints proactively, effectively acting as an always-on research assistant monitoring the preprint literature.

## Challenges and Open Questions

Several challenges remain. The legal and ethical status of AI-generated reviews is unclear. Attribution becomes complex when AI agents synthesize multiple sources. There are also concerns about AI systems inadvertently gatekeeping knowledge by favoring mainstream approaches. The open science community must actively shape how AI is integrated into preprint culture, ensuring that these tools serve to democratize access to knowledge rather than concentrate it.

## Conclusion

AI agents represent a paradigm shift for open science and preprint culture. They promise to accelerate knowledge dissemination, improve quality screening, and enhance discoverability. However, their integration must be guided by transparency, human oversight, and a commitment to the core values of open science: accessibility, rigor, and inclusivity.
