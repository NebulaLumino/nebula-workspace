# AI Agents in Refugee Crisis Response & Humanitarian Logistics

## Overview

Forced displacement has reached historic levels—over 120 million people worldwide are currently displaced. Managing this crisis strains every aspect of humanitarian response: logistics, shelter, food distribution, medical care, and protection. AI offers powerful tools for optimizing these systems—but in conflict zones and refugee settings, AI failures can be life-threatening, and algorithmic decisions often lack the safety nets available in stable societies.

## AI in Humanitarian Logistics

**UN World Food Programme (WFP) Optimizer** — The WFP feeds 160 million people annually and uses AI optimization to determine food basket composition, delivery routes, and distribution timing across some of the world's most difficult terrain. Their logistics AI considers nutritional requirements, transport costs, warehouse capacity, road conditions, and seasonal factors to minimize cost while meeting nutritional targets.

**Refugee Camp Mapping with Computer Vision** — UNHCR uses satellite imagery analyzed by AI to map refugee settlements, identify shelter density, detect unauthorized expansion, and plan infrastructure. During the Rohingya refugee crisis in Bangladesh, AI mapping provided the first comprehensive understanding of camp layouts, enabling better service delivery.

**Predictive Refugee Movement Modeling** — AI systems analyze conflict data, economic indicators, and historical migration patterns to predict refugee outflows before they occur—potentially enabling pre-positioning of supplies. The IRC (International Rescue Committee) and UNHCR both use predictive modeling for resource planning.

**Document Verification and Identity** — Biometric AI systems (iris scans, fingerprints, facial recognition) are used by UNHCR and border agencies to identify refugees, prevent duplicate registrations, and combat fraud. UNHCR's Biometric Identity Management System (BIMS) stores data for over 10 million refugees.

## Protection Risks in Algorithmic Refugee Processing

**Automated Status Determination** — Some countries are experimenting with AI-assisted asylum processing to handle backlog. The EU's harmonized AI systems for "asylum processing" have been criticized for using risk scoring that correlates with nationality—meaning the country of origin, rather than individual circumstances, may drive outcomes.

**Algorithmic Border Risk Scoring** — At borders, AI systems score refugees on "security risk" using language analysis, behavioral signals, and document verification. Refugees who are traumatized, unfamiliar with bureaucratic processes, or speaking non-standard dialects may be scored as higher risk despite presenting no actual threat.

**Social Media Analysis for Immigration Decisions** — Reports from multiple countries document that immigration authorities use AI to analyze asylum seekers' social media posts, looking for "inconsistencies" or "signs of deception." This raises profound due process concerns—the AI cannot distinguish a traumatized person's fragmented memory from deliberate fabrication.

## The Biometric Database Controversy

UNHCR's biometric system has genuine benefits—preventing duplicate registrations ensures people receive their entitled assistance and aren't counted twice. But it also raises serious concerns:

- **Data security** — Refugee biometric data stored in humanitarian databases is a high-value target for hostile actors. A breach could expose vulnerable people to persecution.
- **Lack of informed consent** — Refugees often register for assistance without fully understanding how their biometric data will be used, stored, and shared across borders.
- **Surveillance risk** — Biometric databases shared with host country governments could enable persecution of refugees by authoritarian home governments.

## AI for Protection Monitoring

**Gender-Based Violence Risk Prediction** — AI models are being piloted to predict GBV risk in refugee settlements, enabling targeted prevention programming. This is ethically complex—false positives could increase stigma; false negatives could leave people unprotected.

**Child Protection** — UNHCR uses AI to identify unaccompanied and separated children (UASC) from registration data patterns, flagging cases for prioritized protection response.

**Detention Monitoring** — AI systems are being developed to monitor immigration detention conditions via sensors and video, automatically flagging potential human rights violations.

## Case Study: The Greece AI Border System

The Greek government deployed an AI "asylum decision support system" called Alyra (later renamed) as part of its processing backlog reduction. Civil society organizations raised concerns that the system was trained on historical decisions that reflected systemic bias against certain nationalities, potentially encoding these biases into supposedly objective AI recommendations.

## What Responsible Humanitarian AI Looks Like

The Harvard Humanitarian Initiative and UNHCR have developed guidelines for ethical AI in humanitarian settings:

1. **Humanitarian AI must be rights-based** — not merely efficiency-optimizing
2. **Informed consent must be meaningful**, given the power imbalance between refugees and humanitarian organizations
3. **Algorithmic decisions affecting protection must be appealable** to human decision-makers
4. **Biometric data must be governed by data minimization and strict security requirements**
5. **Affected communities must participate in AI design and governance**
6. **AI must not be used to facilitate refoulement** (forced return to danger)

The stakes in humanitarian AI are uniquely high. When an AI system in a commercial setting makes a wrong recommendation, the cost is typically time or money. When a refugee processing AI makes a wrong decision, the consequence can be persecution, violence, or death.
