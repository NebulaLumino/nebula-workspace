# AI Agents in Climate Modeling & Geoengineering

The intersection of artificial intelligence and climate science represents one of the most consequential applications of modern computing. As climate change accelerates, the need for ever more precise predictive models and emerging geoengineering interventions has created fertile ground for AI agent architectures to demonstrate transformative value.

## The State of Climate Modeling

Traditional climate models—known as General Circulation Models (GCMs)—simulate Earth's atmosphere, oceans, cryosphere, and land surface using physics-based equations. These models, developed over decades, form the backbone of IPCC assessment reports and national climate policy. However, they operate at spatial resolutions too coarse for regional predictions and struggle to capture critical feedback loops like cloud dynamics, permafrost carbon release, and ice sheet collapse.

AI agents are now being deployed in three primary capacities within climate modeling: as _emulators_ that approximate physics-based models at orders-of-magnitude lower computational cost, as _super-resolution_ engines that downscale global projections to actionable local scales, and as _discovery engines_ that identify previously unknown relationships in observational data.

### Emulation and Speed

The most mature application is model emulation. Google's GraphCast, DeepMind's weather prediction models, and academic emulators like EMEC (Earth System Model Emulator) can generate climate projections in seconds that would take traditional supercomputers days or weeks. For decision-makers who need rapid scenario exploration, this represents a paradigm shift. An AI agent designed for climate scenario analysis can autonomously run thousands of simulations testing different emissions pathways, solar radiation management scenarios, or adaptation investment strategies, presenting synthesized policy recommendations rather than raw data.

### AI-Guided Discovery

More profound is AI's emerging role in scientific discovery. Climate models contain parameters—particularly in cloud microphysics and aerosol-cloud interactions—that remain poorly constrained by observations. Reinforcement learning agents are now being used to design optimal field experiment campaigns, directing satellites and sensor networks to maximize information gain. Multi-agent systems coordinate across oceanographic floats, atmospheric monitors, and satellite constellations to autonomously identify and characterize extreme weather events in real time.

## Geoengineering: A New Frontier

Geoengineering—deliberate interventions to cool the planet—remains scientifically and ethically contested, yet AI is becoming essential to its serious evaluation.

### Stratospheric Aerosol Injection (SAI)

The most discussed approach involves injecting reflective aerosols into the stratosphere to mimic volcanic cooling. AI agents are being developed to optimize injection strategies—determining particle size, injection altitude, and geographic targeting to maximize cooling while minimizing disruption to monsoon systems and stratospheric ozone. The governance challenge is enormous: a single country deploying SAI affects the entire planet. Multi-agent negotiation systems are being explored to model international agreements and detect defection scenarios.

### Marine Cloud Brightening

Marine cloud brightening (MCB) involves seeding marine stratocumulus clouds with sea salt particles to increase their reflectivity. This localized approach offers potential reversibility advantages over SAI. AI agents simulate cloud-aerosol interactions at unprecedented fidelity, optimizing sea salt particle injection parameters for specific oceanic regions.

### Carbon Dioxide Removal (CDR)

CDR approaches—direct air capture, ocean alkalinity enhancement, enhanced weathering—require AI-guided monitoring to verify additionality and permanence. AI agents autonomously manage sensor networks at CDR facilities, detect leakage or reversals, and trigger automated responses.

## Open Challenges and Risks

AI climate modeling carries serious risks. Emulators trained on historical data may fail catastrophically under novel climate conditions outside their training distribution—the "foxhole" problem where AI excels in known scenarios but fails precisely when most needed. Model interpretability remains limited; when a climate projection conflicts with physical understanding, it is difficult to diagnose whether the AI has discovered a real phenomenon or is exhibiting a subtle artifact.

## The Path Forward

The most productive architecture appears to be hybrid AI-physics models where AI components are constrained by physical conservation laws, allowing them to learn from data while maintaining physical consistency. Agent-based frameworks that combine multiple specialized models—ocean circulation, atmospheric chemistry, ice dynamics—under a coordinating meta-agent offer a path toward comprehensive Earth system simulation.

Ultimately, AI agents in climate modeling must be understood as decision-support tools embedded in deeply political processes. The model is never neutral; its outputs reflect choices about what to measure, what to optimize, and whose knowledge to privilege. The most important AI contribution to climate science may ultimately be not better predictions, but more transparent and participatory processes for deciding what predictions to generate and how to act on them.
