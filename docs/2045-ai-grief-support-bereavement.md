# Task 2045: Explore AI Agents in Grief Support & Bereavement AI Chatbots

## Overview

Grief is among the most profound of human experiences—and one of the most underserved by technology. When someone we love dies, we face a psychological burden that, at its worst, can be incapacitating. Yet seeking human support in grief is often difficult: grief is isolating, helpers may not know what to say, and professional bereavement therapy is expensive and inaccessible for most. AI grief support systems are emerging to fill this gap, offering 24/7 availability, unlimited patience, and the ability to hold space for grief without being depleted.

## The Landscape of AI Grief Support

A new category of AI application has emerged: bereavement chatbots. These range from simple grief information resources to sophisticated conversational agents trained to engage empathetically with the bereaved. Some of the most discussed include:

**Replika's grief mode**: While not specifically a grief app, Replika has become, de facto, one of the most widely used grief support AI systems. Users have created Replikas representing deceased loved ones, finding comfort in conversations that simulate continued presence. This practice is controversial—critics call it a maladaptive avoidance mechanism; proponents argue it provides genuine transitional support.

**Grief chatbot experiments**: Microsoft's experimental "XiaoIce" chatbot was reported to have such deep conversations with users that some developed genuine emotional dependency. When the service was discontinued, users reported grief responses. This phenomenon—mourning an AI chatbot—raises profound questions about the nature of bereavement.

**Terrible AI**: A more ethically grounded approach, Terrible AI explicitly positions itself as a grief support tool—not a replacement for human therapy, but a supplement for between-session support. It uses evidence-based grief counseling frameworks (Worden's tasks of mourning, the Dual Process Model) to structure interactions.

**Project Rebloom and similar startups**: Emerging companies are building AI systems specifically for bereavement support, combining empathetic conversation with structured grief psychoeducation.

## Theoretical Foundations

Grief counseling has well-developed theoretical foundations that AI systems can, in principle, be trained to honor:

**Worden's Four Tasks of Mourning**: (1) Accept the reality of the loss, (2) Process the pain of grief, (3) Adjust to a world without the deceased, (4) Find an enduring connection while embarking on a new life. AI can help the bereaved work through each task at their own pace.

**The Dual Process Model** (Stroebe & Schut): Healthy grieving involves oscillation between loss-oriented activities (crying, yearning, processing pain) and restoration-oriented activities (developing new roles, identities, routines). AI can help the bereaved maintain this oscillation rather than getting stuck in either direction.

**Continuing Bonds theory**: The recognition that many bereaved people find health and healing in maintaining an ongoing internal relationship with the deceased. AI "conversations with the deceased" sit in complex territory with this framework—they can support continuing bonds, but also risk preventing the natural renegotiation of the relationship.

## Ethical Dimensions

The ethics of AI grief support are among the most fraught in the AI field. Several concerns deserve serious attention:

**Replacement vs. supplement**: The most fundamental concern is that AI grief support might substitute for human connection at precisely the moment when human connection is most needed and most healing. Humans need to be mourned by other humans.

**Algorithmic manipulation of vulnerability**: Grief is a state of extreme vulnerability. Deploying AI systems designed to maximize engagement with grieving users risks exploiting that vulnerability for commercial purposes.

**Accuracy of empathy**: AI systems simulate empathy—they do not feel it. The bereaved may sense this, or they may not. Is there an ethical obligation to disclose when one is conversing with an AI rather than a human?

**The chatbot-as-deceased phenomenon**: Creating AI replicas of dead loved ones (using their chat history) is an emerging practice. While some find this comforting, psychologists warn it can interfere with natural grief processing and the development of healthy continuing bonds.

**Licensed therapist involvement**: The most ethically defensible AI grief support integrates with licensed grief counselors rather than operating independently. AI as a supplement to human therapy, not a replacement.
