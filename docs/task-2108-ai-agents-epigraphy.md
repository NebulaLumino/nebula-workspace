# AI in Epigraphy & Ancient Inscription Analysis

Inscriptions — text carved in stone, stamped on metal, or painted on pottery — are among the most direct voices from the ancient world. Unlike literary texts that were copied and recopied over centuries, inscriptions often survive in their original form, providing datable, geographically anchored evidence of language, law, religion, commerce, and power. Epigraphy, the scholarly study of these texts, is being transformed by artificial intelligence in ways that would have seemed like science fiction a generation ago.

## The Epigrapher's Dilemma: Too Many Stones, Too Few Scholars

The global corpus of ancient inscriptions numbers in the millions: Roman imperial edicts carved into quarry walls from Britain to Syria, Athenian law codes painted on limestone stelae, Byzantine church inscriptions in Greek and Syriac, Maya glyphs on palace facades, Chinese imperial proclamations on bronze vessels. Only a fraction have been published, translated, or even catalogued.

Meanwhile, the number of trained epigraphers capable of reading, dating, and contextualizing inscriptions continues to shrink. AI is being recruited to help bridge this gap — by helping scholars read damaged inscriptions, identify scripts and languages, and place new finds in comparative context more quickly.

## Computer Vision for Damaged Inscriptions

The most immediate AI application in epigraphy is image restoration. Ancient inscriptions are often fragmentary — a stone breaks, a bronze corrodes, a painted inscription weathers away. AI models trained on thousands of inscriptions can generate probabilistic reconstructions of missing characters, suggesting possible completions for lacunae (gaps in the text).

Recent work on Maya glyphs has been particularly striking. Deep learning models trained on complete Classic Maya inscriptions can predict missing glyph blocks in damaged inscriptions with accuracy rates that rival expert epigraphers. The models don't "read" the inscriptions in the human sense — they learn the statistical patterns of Maya orthography and iconography, and use those patterns to fill gaps.

Similar approaches have been applied to Greek inscriptions (where the Packard Humanities Institute's AI system can suggest character completions for fragmentary texts), cuneiform tablets (where AI helps identify partially preserved signs), and archaic Latin inscriptions (where the small corpus makes this particularly challenging).

## Script and Language Identification

AI also helps with the threshold problem: recognizing that a stone bears an inscription, identifying what script it uses, and determining what language it's written in — before any translation work begins. Computer vision models trained on the full corpus of known scripts (Latin, Greek, Hebrew, Nabataean, Sogdian, Old Turkic, Maya, Egyptian hieroglyphs, cuneiform, and dozens more) can classify an unknown inscription in seconds.

This is more than convenience: for archaeologists working in the field, having a preliminary script identification before an inscription is even lifted from the ground can determine excavation strategy, conservation priorities, and which specialist to consult.

## Semantic Analysis and Historical Context

Beyond decipherment, AI enables semantic analysis at scale. When scholars can process thousands of inscriptions computationally rather than by hand, patterns emerge that individual reading cannot reveal: the vocabulary of imperial ideology, the evolution of legal terminology, the geographic distribution of religious terminology, the social language of trade.

Topic modeling, word embedding, and transformer-based NLP approaches — techniques developed for modern languages — are now being applied to corpus epigraphy. The results are reshaping our understanding of ancient social and political life: how Roman emperors communicated with provincial elites, how Greek city-states framed civic identity, how Buddhist donors in ancient India described their religious motivations.

## Detecting Forgery and Authentication

One of the most practically important AI applications in epigraphy is forgery detection. The antiquities market has always attracted forgers, and high-value inscriptions — especially those with religious or historical significance — are frequently faked or "improved" (real but unremarkable inscriptions altered to make them more valuable).

AI models trained on the physical and linguistic characteristics of genuine inscriptions can flag suspicious specimens. A forged inscription may reproduce the letterforms of a genuine example but get the grammatical constructions wrong — AI catches these inconsistencies. Similarly, the material science of the inscription (stone type, weathering patterns, patina composition) can be analyzed computationally to assess authenticity.

## The LIMC and Beyond: Building Digital Epigraphic Corpus

The ultimate goal of AI epigraphy is not to replace human scholars but to enable a fully searchable, computable global corpus of ancient inscriptions. Projects like the Inscriptions of the Late Antiquity (IGLS), the Packard Humanities Institute's Searchable Greek Inscriptions, and the Epigraphic Database Roma are laying the groundwork. AI will be the key to making these databases interoperable, cross-referencing inscriptions across languages and scripts, and surfacing patterns across the global corpus.
