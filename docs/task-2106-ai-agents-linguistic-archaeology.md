# AI in Linguistic Archaeology & Historical Language Reconstruction

Language is the most ephemeral artifact humans leave behind — and the most revealing. As societies rise, fracture, merge, and collapse, their languages evolve, branch into new tongues, or vanish entirely. Artificial intelligence is now enabling scholars to reconstruct extinct languages, decode forgotten scripts, and trace the deep prehistory of human communication with unprecedented precision.

## The Comparative Method and Its Computational Limits

The traditional tool of historical linguistics is the comparative method — identifying regular sound correspondences between related languages to reconstruct their common ancestor. Proto-Indo-European, the reconstructed ancestor of languages from Sanskrit to English, was painstakingly reconstructed over two centuries of painstaking comparative work by scholars like August Schlegel and Ferdinand de Saussure.

AI extends this methodology computationally. Neural sequence-to-sequence models trained on pairs of related languages can generate probabilistic reconstructions of proto-forms, identifying sound correspondences that human analysts might miss. Critically, AI doesn't replace the comparative method — it accelerates and systematizes it, processing thousands of cognate pairs across dozens of languages simultaneously.

## Computational Phylogenetics: Languages as Trees

The most ambitious application of AI to historical linguistics is computational phylogenetics — treating language evolution like biological evolution, with sound changes as "mutations" and language splits as speciation events. Bayesian phylogenetics models, similar to those used in evolutionary biology, can estimate when languages diverged, given enough cognate data.

Recent work by quantitative linguists has applied these methods to the controversial question of Proto-Indo-European origins, generating date estimates for the Proto-Indo-European homeland that align with archaeological evidence for steppe migrations circa 4500-2500 BCE. AI models have also been used to argue against long-standing " Anatolian" hypotheses, suggesting instead that Indo-European spread with Yamnaya steppe pastoralists.

## Deciphering Unknown Scripts

Perhaps the most dramatic AI contribution to linguistic archaeology is in decipherment. Scripts like Linear A (Minoan Crete, still undeciphered), Proto-Sinaitic (ancient Semitic script), and the Voynich Manuscript have resisted decipherment for generations. AI is changing the equation.

Transformer models trained on known ancient scripts can generate probabilistic mappings of symbol sequences in unknown scripts, hypothesizing phonetic values, word boundaries, and grammatical structures. In 2023, researchers used a BERT-style model to propose a Proto-Sinaitic reading of several undeciphered symbols in a Canaanite inscription — a proof of concept that AI can generate actionable decipherment hypotheses, even if human expertise remains essential for validation.

## NLP for Historical Documents

Beyond reconstruction, AI-powered natural language processing is transforming how historians work with texts in languages they can read. Neural machine translation models trained on parallel corpora of ancient Greek, Latin, Arabic, and Chinese texts can now produce rough draft translations that human scholars refine rather than produce from scratch.

More advanced models handle dead languages specifically: Latin-specific BERT variants, Akkadian GPT models, and Classical Chinese NLP pipelines. These models are trained not just on clean literary texts but on administrative documents, graffiti, and letters — the raw material of everyday life in ancient societies.

## Language Death and Revitalization

AI also serves contemporary communities facing language extinction. Low-resource language NLP — the challenge of building AI language tools for tongues with few documented examples — has emerged as a critical research area. AI can help document endangered languages by generating phonetic transcriptions of audio recordings, predicting grammatical structures from limited examples, and creating learning materials for revitalization programs.

The result is a strange inversion: AI, trained primarily on the world's most data-rich languages, is being redeployed to preserve the most data-poor — languages that exist in the mouths of only a few elderly speakers, with no writing tradition and no digitized corpus.
