## Overview

Somatic psychology is a branch of mind-body psychotherapy that focuses on the body's role in storing, processing, and releasing traumatic experiences. The word "somatic" derives from the Greek *sōma*, meaning "body." Unlike purely cognitive therapies that treat the mind as the primary locus of psychological distress, somatic approaches hold that traumatic memory is stored not just as narrative recall but as physiological patterns in the body—muscle tension, frozen postures, disrupted breathing, dysregulated autonomic arousal, and altered pain thresholds.

The foundational insight of somatic psychology is that the body keeps the score: Bessel van der Kolk's landmark 2014 book popularized this phrase, but the theoretical lineage runs through Wilhelm Reich (who first linked character armor to unconscious conflict), Pierre Janet (who observed that traumatic memories fail to integrate narratively), and more recently Peter Levine (Somatic Experiencing), Pat Ogden (Sensorimotor Psychotherapy), and Stephen Porges (Polyvagal Theory).

### Core Theoretical Constructs

**Trauma is stored procedurally, not just declaratively.** Procedural memory encodes how to do things (ride a bike, flinch from a loud noise) and how the body responds to threat. Traumatic events that are overwhelming—where fight/flight/freeze responses are insufficiently discharged—become "stuck" in procedural memory, replaying as somatic symptoms without conscious narrative access.

**The window of tolerance.** Dan Siegel's concept describes the optimal arousal zone (hyper-arousal to hypo-arousal) within which a person can process emotional material without becoming overwhelmed. Trauma narrows the window; somatic approaches aim to expand it through interoceptive awareness and co-regulation.

**Polyvagal Theory (Porges, 1995).** The autonomic nervous system has three components: ventral vagal (social engagement, calm state), sympathetic (fight/flight), and dorsal vagal (freeze, shutdown, collapse). Trauma often shifts the system toward dorsal vagal or sympathetic dominance. Somatic interventions target vagal tone to restore ventral vagal regulation.

**Discharge and completion of defensive responses.** When an animal is threatened and successfully flees or fights, the defensive response completes naturally and the nervous system returns to equilibrium. Humans in overwhelming situations often cannot complete these responses—we freeze, we are prevented from fighting back, or escape routes are unavailable. Somatic therapies help people complete these responses somatically (in safe conditions) to release stored survival energy.

**Grounding and interoception.** Central to somatic work is developing interoceptive awareness—the capacity to perceive and interpret bodily sensations. Many trauma survivors are dissociated from their bodies (alexithymia, depersonalization). Somatic therapies build the capacity to notice body states without being overwhelmed by them.

## AI Applications

### AI-Assisted Somatic Psychoeducation

LLMs can explain somatic concepts to clients in accessible, non-threatening language. A trauma-informed AI coach can introduce concepts like window of tolerance, polyvagal states, and body keep score gradually, allowing clients to learn at their own pace without triggering dysregulation. The AI can check in: "Based on what you just described (difficulty sleeping, feeling heaviness in your chest), you may be experiencing a dorsal vagal state. Would you like to try a brief grounding exercise?"

**Prompt application:** "You are a somatic trauma specialist. Explain polyvagal theory to a client who has experienced chronic stress. Use simple, non-technical language. Ask if they'd like to try a brief body awareness exercise. After they respond, guide them through a simple grounding practice: feel your feet on the floor, name 3 sounds you hear, notice your breath."

### Virtual Co-Regulation Partner

Polyvagal-informed interventions often require co-regulation—a calm, present other person whose nervous system helps regulate the client's. AI companions (voice or text) trained in polyvagal principles could serve as virtual co-regulators for low-intensity distress, providing: paced breathing prompts, orienting cues ("notice what's around you right now"), prosodic softening, and gentle titration of distress tolerance.

### Somatic Session Note Analysis

Clinicians can use AI to analyze session transcripts and identify somatic markers—moments where the client describes body states, where arousal shifts occur, where interventions were applied. AI can flag patterns across sessions: "Client consistently reports heaviness in chest and shallow breathing when discussing [topic]. This may indicate somatic activation. Consider adding somatic grounding before proceeding."

### Wearable Integration

Integration with wearable devices (heart rate variability monitors, EDA sensors, Oura rings) allows AI to track autonomic arousal markers in real time. A somatic AI coach could receive HRV data and respond: "Your resting HRV has been elevated since yesterday, suggesting your nervous system is under stress. This aligns with what you described about last night's sleep quality. Would you like a brief somatic resourcing practice?" This bridges passive monitoring with active somatic intervention.

### Assessment and Tracking

AI tools can conduct initial somatic assessments via conversation: inquiring about common somatic trauma manifestations (chronic pain, dissociation, startle responses, sleep disturbance, digestive issues, sexual dysfunction). Tracking these over time can reveal patterns and inform treatment planning.

**Prompt application:** "Conduct a somatic trauma screening interview. Ask about: (1) common trauma body symptoms (chronic tension, pain, numbness), (2) sleep quality and dreams, (3) startle response, (4) capacity to feel emotions in the body, (5) dissociative episodes. Use warm, non-pathologizing language. After assessment, summarize findings and suggest 3 gentle somatic practices appropriate for their current state."

## Tools & Methods

### Somatic Experiencing (SE) — Peter Levine
- **Core:** Focus on tracking bodily sensations to identify and complete frozen defensive responses
- **Method:** "Pendulation"—oscillating between areas of sensation and resource (felt sense of safety)
- **Key technique:** SIBAM (Sensation, Image, Behavior, Affect, Meaning)
- **Phase-oriented:** Tracking resources → containing disruption → discharge and completion

### Sensorimotor Psychotherapy (Ogden)
- **Core:** Integrates body-based interventions with cognitive and emotional processing
- **Method:** Stage 1—process frozen defensive responses; Stage 2—discharge activation; Stage 3—complete defensive responses and consolidate learning
- **Key technique:** "Movement repertoire"—exploring new, post-trauma movement options

### EMDR (Eye Movement Desensitization and Reprocessing) — Shapiro
- **Core:** Bilateral stimulation (eye movements, tactile) aids processing of traumatic material
- **Method:** 8-phase protocol; imaginal reprocessing with bilateral stimulation
- **Body component:** Body scan to identify somatic elements of traumatic memories

### Internal Family Systems (IFS) — Schwartz
- **Core:** The psyche contains discrete "parts" with their own roles and burdens; work to access a calm "Self"
- **Somatic connection:** "Mind-to-organism communication"; tracking where parts manifest in the body
- **Method:** Parts mapping → unburdening → integration

### Breathwork Modalities
- **Somatic breathwork:** Holotropic breathwork (Grof), rebirthing, biodynamic breathwork
- **Mechanism:** Increased oxygenation, CO2 tolerance, vagal stimulation through breath
- **Contraindications:** Must screen for cardiovascular conditions, pregnancy, severe dissociation, psychosis

### Body-Based Mindfulness
- Body scan meditation (Kabat-Zinn's MBSR)
- Progressive muscular relaxation (Jacobson)
- Feldenkrais Method, Alexander Technique
- Yoga trauma-sensitive practices (e.g., Trauma Center Trauma-Sensitive Yoga)

### Biofeedback and Neurofeedback
- **HRV biofeedback:** Training vagal tone through real-time heart rate variability display
- **Neurofeedback:** EEG training to increase alpha/theta ratio, reduce hyperarousal

## Challenges

**Trauma is embodied—AI cannot feel it.** A core limitation: somatic therapy requires a lived, feeling body. AI can describe sensations, guide practices, and track patterns, but cannot model the felt sense the way a trained somatic therapist can. AI's primary role is supportive, not therapeutic.

**Risk of triggering without co-regulation.** Guiding someone through a somatic discharge exercise without a human co-regulator present can be dangerous for severely traumatized individuals. Discharged traumatic material can overwhelm a person alone without support. AI tools must build in safety guardrails (screening for severe trauma, escalation warnings, crisis resources) and clarify that AI somatic tools are not therapy.

**Evidence base is still developing.** While EMDR has robust RCT evidence, other somatic modalities (Somatic Experiencing, Sensorimotor Psychotherapy) have fewer large-scale controlled trials. More research is needed to establish which somatic techniques work best for which presentations.

**Training standards vary.** The somatic therapy field has variable training standards. "Somatic" practitioners may have very different backgrounds (psychotherapy, massage therapy, yoga instruction). AI tools built on somatic frameworks need clear disclaimers about the level of training of the human reviewing AI recommendations.

**Cultural differences in body relationship.** Concepts like interoception and body-based awareness are Western constructs. Somatic techniques developed in Western clinical contexts may have limited cross-cultural applicability. Some cultures have different relationships to body disclosure, touch, breathwork, and emotional expression.

**Integration with talk therapy.** Many clients are in traditional talk therapy in addition to somatic work. Coordination between modalities is important. AI tools could facilitate this but must be careful not to recommend somatic techniques that conflict with or contradict a client's established therapeutic relationship.

## Ethics

**Crisis and safety.** Somatic distress can be severe. AI must have clear escalation protocols: if a user describes suicidal ideation, active panic, or dissociation, the AI must provide crisis resources and decline to guide somatic exercises. Somatic releasing can destabilize.

**Scope of practice.** AI tools providing "somatic coaching" must be clear that they are psychoeducational, not therapeutic. They cannot replace a trained somatic psychotherapist. Clear scope of practice statements and referrals to licensed clinicians are required.

**Informed consent for body-based practices.** Even simple breathwork carries risks for certain medical conditions. AI tools guiding body-based practices should include screening questions and clear contraindications.

**Trauma-informed design principles.** AI interfaces themselves should be trauma-informed: no dark patterns, no manipulative engagement tactics, no nudges toward content that could be destabilizing. The interface should feel safe, containable, and non-threatening.

**Avoiding touch-related guidance.** Somatic therapy often involves skilled touch (from a trained therapist). AI must never guide physical touch interventions without human supervision, as improper touch can re-traumatize.

## Future Directions

### AI-Enhanced Polyvagal Monitoring

Integration with wearable devices will allow real-time polyvagal state inference from HRV, skin conductance, and respiratory patterns. AI can detect when a user's autonomic state is shifting toward dorsal vagal (shutdown) or sympathetic (hyperarousal) dominance and intervene with appropriate grounding or activation exercises.

### Personalized Somatic Intervention Matching

Different bodies respond differently to somatic interventions. Some clients benefit from vigorous movement and breathwork; others from extremely gentle, slow practices. AI can learn over time which somatic modalities a client responds to best and personalize recommendations based on reported outcomes.

### VR Somatic Environments

Virtual reality can create contained, safe environments for somatic processing—controlled stimuli, graduated exposure, social engagement cues calibrated to the client's window of tolerance. Combined with AI guidance, VR somatic therapy is an active research frontier.

### Integration with Psychedelic-Assisted Therapy

Somatic approaches are increasingly integrated with psychedelic-assisted therapy (psilocybin, MDMA). These substances often produce profound somatic states; somatic therapists guide clients through embodied experiences during and after sessions. AI could assist in session preparation and integration, though not during active dosing.

### Somatic AI for Chronic Pain Populations

Chronic pain and trauma are deeply interconnected. Somatic approaches to chronic pain (Trauma-Sensitive Yoga, Somatic Experiencing) are showing promise. AI tools can provide accessible somatic pain management education and practices for the large chronic pain population who may not have access to specialist somatic therapists.

### Computational Models of Embodiment

Advanced AI research is exploring computational models of embodied cognition—understanding how agent-environment interaction shapes cognition. This could eventually enable more sophisticated AI understanding of how body states influence cognitive and emotional processing, better matching human-like interoceptive reasoning.
