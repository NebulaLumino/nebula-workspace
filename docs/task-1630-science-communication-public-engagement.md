# Task 1630: Explore AI Agents in Science Communication & Public Engagement AI

## Overview

Science communication—the practice of conveying scientific findings to non-expert audiences—is one of the most important and most neglected activities in science. The gap between scientific knowledge and public understanding has significant consequences: it affects policy decisions on climate, health, and technology; it shapes public trust in scientific institutions; and it determines whether societies can respond effectively to complex challenges like pandemics, AI risks, and environmental degradation.

AI agents are beginning to transform science communication at every level, from translating complex findings into accessible language to personalizing science content for different audiences to detecting and countering scientific misinformation.

## The Science Communication Challenge

Effective science communication requires not just translating technical language but understanding the audience's prior knowledge, values, and concerns. A message about vaccine safety that resonates with one community may be counterproductive with another. This requires nuance, cultural sensitivity, and the ability to adapt messaging to context—qualities that AI systems are increasingly capable of.

The scale of modern scientific output makes it impossible for human communicators alone to keep pace. Over 3 million scientific papers are published each year—far more than any science communicator could read, let alone synthesize and explain. AI agents can serve as intelligent filters and synthesizers, identifying the most significant findings and presenting them in accessible formats.

## AI as Science Interpreter

Large language models can read and synthesize scientific papers, extracting key findings and presenting them in plain language. This is more than simple summarization—it requires understanding the significance of results in the context of the field, recognizing limitations and caveats, and translating technical terminology into accessible alternatives without losing precision.

AI systems like Elicit, Scispace, and various science-focused chatbots are already being used by journalists, policymakers, and the public to understand scientific topics. More sophisticated agents could maintain continuous monitoring of preprints and journal articles in specific fields, producing regular briefings that synthesize the latest developments for different audiences.

## Personalizing Science for Audiences

Different audiences need different framings of the same science. A climate scientist, a policymaker, a farmer, and a teenager in an urban area may all need accurate information about climate change but require different levels of technical detail, different examples, and different calls to action.

AI agents can personalize science content by modeling audience characteristics and adapting both content and framing accordingly. This goes beyond simple readability adjustments to genuine audience modeling—understanding what prior knowledge to assume, what concerns are most salient, and what communication style is most effective.

## Visual Communication and Data Visualization

Much of science communication involves data—charts, graphs, maps, and diagrams. AI agents can automatically generate appropriate visualizations from data, create animated explainers of dynamic processes (like climate models or cellular biology), and adapt visualizations for different platforms and audiences.

More sophisticated systems can take a scientific paper, extract its key figures and data, and produce a complete multimedia explainer with voice-over, captions, and supplementary text. This could dramatically lower the cost of producing high-quality science content.

## Detecting and Countering Misinformation

Scientific misinformation—false or misleading claims about scientific topics—spreads rapidly online and can have life-threatening consequences (as seen with vaccine hesitancy and COVID-19 misinformation). AI agents can monitor social media and news for scientific misinformation, assess its accuracy against the scientific literature, and generate fact-checking responses.

However, research has shown that simply providing factual corrections is often insufficient—the "misinformation overkill effect" can actually reinforce false beliefs. AI systems designed to counter misinformation must be carefully designed to acknowledge uncertainty, build trust, and present corrections in ways that are more compelling than the original misinformation.

## Science Journalism and Automated Reporting

AI agents are being used to assist science journalists by identifying significant papers, extracting key findings, and suggesting angles for stories. In some cases, AI systems generate first-draft news articles from press releases and papers, with human journalists providing verification and editorial judgment.

This raises important questions about the future of science journalism: if AI can produce accurate summaries of scientific findings, what unique value do human journalists provide? The answer likely lies in investigative work, context, critical analysis, and the ability to build trust with sources and audiences—areas where AI is still weak.

## Public Engagement and Dialogue

Beyond one-way communication, AI agents can facilitate genuine dialogue between scientists and publics. Chatbots and conversational agents can answer questions about science topics, engage with concerns and misconceptions, and direct people to reliable resources. These systems can operate at a scale and availability that no human team could match.

The key challenge is ensuring that these systems are trustworthy and accurate—relying on AI for public engagement requires robust guardrails against hallucination and manipulation.

## Ethical Considerations

AI-generated science communication raises important ethical questions. Who is responsible when an AI system misrepresents a scientific finding? How should uncertainty be communicated to avoid both false confidence and unnecessary doubt? How can we ensure that AI communication tools serve the public interest rather than the interests of whoever controls them?

These questions require ongoing attention from scientists, technologists, policymakers, and the public.

## Conclusion

AI agents are transforming science communication from a human-intensive craft into a scalable, personalized, data-driven practice. They can synthesize vast scientific literature, personalize content for different audiences, generate visualizations, detect misinformation, and facilitate dialogue between science and society. The most powerful applications combine AI efficiency with human judgment, trust-building, and ethical oversight. As AI capabilities continue to improve, the challenge is not whether AI will transform science communication but whether it will do so in ways that strengthen rather than undermine public understanding of and trust in science.
