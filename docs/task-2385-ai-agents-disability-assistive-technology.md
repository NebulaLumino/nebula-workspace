# Task 2385: Explore AI Agents in Disability, Assistive Technology & Accessibility AI

## Overview
AI is one of the most genuinely liberating technologies for people with disabilities — enabling communication, mobility, and independence that was previously impossible. But it's also an area where hype often outpaces real-world benefit.

## Key Applications
- **Speech-to-text for deaf/hard-of-hearing**: Real-time captioning and transcription
- **Image description for blind/low-vision**: AI describes images and videos aloud
- **Wheelchair navigation AI**: Autonomous wheelchair obstacle avoidance
- **Communication devices**: AI AAC (augmentative and alternative communication) for nonverbal people
- **Cognitive assistance**: AI reminders, scheduling, and task guidance for people with cognitive disabilities
- **Bionic limbs**: AI-controlled prosthetic limbs with sensory feedback

## Tools & Frameworks
- **Apple (Accessibility features)**: VoiceOver, Live Captions, Door Detection
- **Google Lookout**: AI image description for blind users
- **Microsoft Seeing AI**: Comprehensive AI app for blind users
- **TapTalk**: AAC communication app
- **CALA (body-worn AI)**: AI assistants for mobility impairments
- **BrainGate (neural interface)**: AI translating brain signals to text/robot control

## Ethical Considerations
- **Access inequality**: Advanced AI accessibility tools are expensive
- **Dignity vs. efficiency**: Some accessibility tools can feel clinical or reduce user agency
- **Algorithmic bias**: AI systems trained primarily on non-disabled users may fail disabled users
- **Over-reliance**: AI dependency may reduce development of human skills
- **Data privacy**: Disability status is highly sensitive; breaches are particularly harmful

## Key Players
Apple, Google, Microsoft, assistive technology companies, BrainGate, academic HCI labs
