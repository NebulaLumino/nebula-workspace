# AI Agents in Climate Engineering & Geoengineering

## Current State

Climate engineering—deliberate interventions to counteract anthropogenic climate change—encompasses both carbon removal and solar radiation management (SRM). AI agents are being applied across the spectrum: optimizing direct air capture processes, modeling ocean iron fertilization impacts, coordinating stratospheric aerosol injection (SAI) delivery systems, and managing afforestation at scale. The IPCC's 2021 report acknowledged geoengineering as a potential supplement to emissions cuts. While no large-scale SRM deployment has occurred, AI-enabled research is reducing uncertainty around efficacy, risks, and side effects. The governance challenge—how humanity collectively decides to deploy planetary-scale interventions—is increasingly urgent.

## Key Use Cases

AI agents optimize carbon capture materials and processes, modeling millions of potential chemical configurations for enhanced rock weathering or bioenergy with carbon capture. For marine cloud brightening, agents model aerosol interactions with clouds and ocean biogeochemistry. In afforestation, AI coordinates seed selection, planting patterns, and monitoring across continental scales using satellite imagery and drone surveys. Climate modeling agents run massively parallel simulations testing different geoengineering scenarios, including termination shock risks. Decision-support agents help policymakers weigh geoengineering options against mitigation alternatives. Emerging AI platforms model the cascading effects of interventions across weather systems, agriculture, and geopolitics.

## Technical Challenges

Climate systems are chaotic and interconnected—interventions produce nonlocal effects that are difficult to predict. AI models trained on historical climate data may fail under geoengineered conditions outside that distribution. Scale is extreme: meaningful SRM would require coordinating thousands of aircraft flights delivering aerosols at altitude. The termination shock problem—rapid warming if SRM suddenly stops—is particularly difficult to model. Ethical governance frameworks must be developed before deployment, not after. The asymmetric risk profile (global benefits, regional harms) creates geopolitical tensions that no technical system can resolve.

## Ethical & Safety Considerations

Solar geoengineering could alter monsoon patterns, harming billions who depend on them, while benefiting others. unilateral deployment by one nation or corporation would impose risks on others without consent. Geoengineering may reduce pressure to cut emissions—the "moral hazard" problem. The appearance of a technological fix could undermine climate social movements. There is no existing international governance body with authority over planetary-scale interventions. AI agents making decisions with planetary consequences raise fundamental questions about technological sovereignty and democratic deliberation.

## Future Outlook

The case for climate engineering grows stronger as emissions cuts remain insufficient. Within 10-20 years, we may see small-scale field experiments in SAI, with AI playing central roles in monitoring and coordination. Carbon removal at gigaton scale requires AI-optimized industrial processes. The governance vacuum must be filled by international bodies with real enforcement power. The deeper lesson geoengineering offers is that humanity is developing the capacity to deliberately engineer planetary systems—a capability that extends far beyond climate and demands new forms of global coordination and ethical reflection.
