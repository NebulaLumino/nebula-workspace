# AI Agents in Scientific Peer Review

## Overview

Peer review is the backbone of scientific publishing — and also one of its most strained processes. The volume of scientific submissions has grown exponentially while the pool of qualified reviewers has not. AI agents are now being deployed at nearly every stage of the peer review pipeline, raising profound questions about the future of scientific quality control.

## Manuscript Quality Screening

Before a manuscript reaches peer reviewers, journals increasingly use AI screening to assess basic quality and fit. Tools like Elsevier's Artemis and Frontiers' AI Reviewers scan for statistical rigor, novelty signals, methodology clarity, and alignment with journal scope. Papers that fail automated screening are returned to authors with flagged issues — a process that previously would have consumed expert reviewer time.

The value is clear: reducing reviewer burden on clear rejections. The risk is that automated quality signals may systematically disadvantage novel or methodologically unusual research that does not fit expected patterns.

## Automated Reviewer Matching

The reviewer assignment problem — matching manuscript topics to qualified reviewers — is a classic recommendation challenge. Platforms like PubMed's reviewer suggestion tools and commercial services like ReviewerLocator use expertise matching algorithms to identify appropriate reviewers. The best systems combine publication history analysis, citation patterns, and self-reported expertise with natural language matching on manuscript abstracts.

Some journals are experimenting with AI agents that proactively suggest reviewer candidates without editorial prompting, operating as an always-on matching service.

## Detecting Paper Mills and Fraudulent Research

Perhaps the most consequential AI application in peer review is fraud detection. "Paper mills" — organizations that produce fabricated manuscripts on demand — have become a significant threat to scientific integrity. AI systems are now being trained on known paper mill characteristics: statistically improbable results, unusual image duplications, inconsistent writing style across sections, and author signature patterns.

COPE (Committee on Publication Ethics) has published guidelines recommending AI-assisted screening, and journals are actively deploying tools. The cat-and-mouse dynamic here is intense: as AI detection improves, fraudulent production will likely also adopt AI assistance.

## Statistical and Methodological Review

AI systems are being trained to check statistical methods with increasing sophistication — detecting common p-hacking indicators, checking for appropriate power calculations, verifying that statistical tests match the data distributions described, and flagging claims that exceed what the data supports.

This goes beyond simple rules (e.g., "must report effect sizes") to model-based assessment of methodological soundness. Nature journals and some specialty publications are piloting AI-assisted statistical review.

## The Assist vs. Replace Debate

The most contentious question in the field: should AI agents conduct peer review, or only assist human reviewers? Thepurist position holds that only expert humans can assess the full significance and rigor of scientific work. The pragmatist position notes that human reviewers routinely miss errors, take months to deliver reviews, and are subject to bias and fatigue.

Most journals adopting AI are in the "assist" camp — AI flags issues, humans decide. But the practical pressure to speed up review timelines is pushing some toward more autonomous AI involvement.

## Key Organizations

- **Elsevier** — AI manuscript screening, reviewer matching, plagiarism detection
- **Frontiers** — AI Reviewer platform, structured review formats
- **ResearchSquare** — preprint and manuscript AI review tools
- **COPE** — ethics guidelines for AI in peer review
- **CaseText (RELX)** — legal AI parallel to scientific review tools

## Outlook

The trajectory is clear: AI will handle an increasing fraction of the mechanical aspects of peer review (format checking, methodological screening, reviewer matching), while humans retain final judgment on significance, novelty, and interpretational questions. The most disruptive possibility is not AI replacing reviewers, but AI agents writing papers — at which point the entire review pipeline faces a fundamental identity crisis.

---

**Key Insight:** Scientific peer review is one of the last domains where human expert judgment is considered irreplaceable. The pressure from paper volume and the improving capabilities of AI are on a collision course. The resolution will shape how science validates itself for decades.
