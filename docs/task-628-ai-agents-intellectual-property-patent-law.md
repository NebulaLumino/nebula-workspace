# AI Agents in Intellectual Property & Patent Law

Intellectual property law—patents, trademarks, copyrights, and trade secrets—is being transformed by artificial intelligence in ways that challenge centuries-old legal doctrines. AI can now search millions of documents in seconds, generate draft patent claims, and monitor global trademark activity in real-time. But it also creates new legal puzzles about authorship, ownership, and what "invention" even means.

## AI-Powered Patent Search and Prior Art

Patent prosecution begins with prior art search—finding existing inventions similar to the one being claimed. Traditional searches required attorneys to manually review databases, a slow and expensive process. AI-powered patent search tools like Patscope (Clarivate), Orbit Intelligence, and Thomson Reuters KeyPerspectives use natural language processing and semantic similarity to find relevant prior art in seconds. These systems understand the technical meaning of patent claims, not just keyword matching, making them dramatically more effective.

More sophisticated systems can predict patent grant likelihood, assess patent portfolio value, and identify whitespace opportunities—areas where innovation is sparse and patent protection might be uniquely valuable. For corporations managing large patent portfolios, AI tools like Anaqua and IPfolio provide portfolio optimization suggestions based on competitive intelligence.

## AI-Assisted Patent Drafting

Generative AI is now being used to draft patent applications. Tools like Scholarcy and Iris help attorneys generate claim language and specification text from technical descriptions. In 2024, the USPTO issued guidance on AI-assisted inventions, requiring disclosure of AI contributions to patent applications. The key question is how much human involvement is required for an invention to be "made by" a human inventor.

## The DABUS Case: AI as Inventor

The most legally significant AI patent case involved Stephen Thaler and his AI system DABUS (Device for the Autonomous Bootstrapping of Unified Sentience). Thaler attempted to register patents where DABUS was listed as the sole inventor. Courts in the US, UK, Australia, and Europe uniformly rejected this, holding that only natural persons can be inventors under current law. These rulings have been criticized by some IP scholars as lagging behind technological reality—eventually, legal systems will need to address whether and how AI-generated inventions should be treated.

## Copyright and Generative AI

The copyright implications of generative AI are being litigated intensely. The core question is whether training AI models on copyrighted works constitutes infringement, and who owns the output. Multiple class action lawsuits (Andersen v. Stability AI, Getty Images v. Stability AI, and others) are working through these questions. The US Copyright Office has ruled that AI-generated images without human creative control are not copyrightable, but human-curated AI outputs may receive protection.

Content ID systems—a form of AI matching—are now critical infrastructure for rights holders. YouTube's Content ID processes over 500 years of video content daily, automatically identifying copyrighted music, film, and TV content. This system generates significant revenue for rights holders but has also been criticized for over-blocking fair use content and legitimate criticism.

## Trademark Monitoring

Global trademark monitoring uses AI to scan registration databases and domain registrations for potential infringement. Systems like trademark clearance AI can search across hundreds of jurisdictions simultaneously, identifying confusingly similar marks before they cause consumer harm. This represents a significant efficiency gain for trademark attorneys and brand protection teams.

The IP law AI landscape is evolving faster than the legal framework can adapt. The fundamental questions—who owns AI-generated output, can AI be an inventor, how should AI-trained-on-copyrighted-works be treated—are likely to require legislative solutions rather than case-by-case adjudication. For practitioners, AI is simultaneously a productivity tool and a source of new legal complexity.
