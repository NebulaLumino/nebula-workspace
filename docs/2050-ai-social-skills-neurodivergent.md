# Task 2050: Explore AI Agents in Social Skills Training for Neurodivergent Individuals AI

## Overview

Neurodivergence—encompassing autism spectrum conditions, ADHD, dyslexia, and other cognitive differences—is not a deficit to be corrected but a different cognitive operating system. Yet neurodivergent individuals often face genuine challenges navigating social environments built for neurotypical cognition. AI agents are emerging as potentially transformative tools for social skills training: patient, non-judgmental, endlessly available practice partners for social situations that feel dangerous or confusing. This task explores both the genuine promise and the ethical complexities of this application.

## Social Skills Training: Traditional Approaches

Social skills training (SST) for autism and related conditions has traditionally relied on:
- **Direct instruction**: Explicit teaching of social rules (e.g., "maintain eye contact for 3-5 seconds")
- **Role-play with therapists**: Practicing conversations in controlled settings
- **Social stories**: Narrative scripts for navigating specific social situations
- **Video modeling**: Watching and imitating social interactions

These approaches work for many individuals but have significant limitations: human trainers are expensive and not available 24/7; role-play with humans can be high-anxiety for those who struggle with social performance; the generalization from training to real-world situations is notoriously difficult to achieve.

## AI as Practice Partner

AI systems offer several theoretical advantages for social skills training:

**Low stakes**: Practicing a difficult conversation with an AI carries none of the social risk of practicing with a human. For individuals with social anxiety—common among autistics and those with ADHD—anxiety about evaluation can block learning. AI provides a genuinely safe practice environment.

**Patience and repetition**: Human trainers tire; AI does not. The same scenario can be practiced 50 times without the trainer becoming frustrated or patronizing. For autistic individuals who need extensive repetition to consolidate learning, this is transformative.

**Personalization**: AI can adapt to the specific profile of the learner—their particular social challenges, their interests, their communication style. No human trainer can provide this level of individualized adaptation.

**Explicit rule teaching**: Many autistic individuals learn social rules best through explicit articulation rather than implicit social observation. AI can directly teach the "why" behind social conventions that neurotypical people absorb unconsciously: why do we make small talk before getting to business? Why do people orient toward each other physically when they want to signal engagement?

## Specific Applications

**Autism and social cognition**: AI systems like Apple's AskSAMAR, and research systems at Carnegie Mellon and Stanford, use conversational AI to teach social scripts for employment interviews, dating, and workplace interactions. These systems provide feedback on what to say and how to interpret responses.

**ADHD and executive function coaching**: For individuals with ADHD, the social challenges often relate to executive function (interrupting, not reading social cues about when to stop talking). AI coaching systems can provide real-time gentle prompts and post-conversation debriefs.

**Social anxiety CBT**: AI chatbots designed for social anxiety provide cognitive-behavioral interventions—identifying and challenging distorted thoughts about social situations, graduated exposure planning—available at 3 AM when anxiety spikes.

**Pragmatic language coaching**: For individuals whose challenge is not understanding language but using it appropriately in context (pragmatic language disorder), AI can provide feedback on whether a communication is appropriate for the social context.

## Ethical Tensions

The use of AI to train neurodivergent individuals in "normal" social behavior raises genuine ethical tensions:

**Normalizing vs. authentic**: Is the goal to help neurodivergent individuals develop skills that allow them to participate authentically in the social world on their own terms, or to make them more comfortable for the neurotypicals around them? These may be very different goals requiring very different AI designs.

**The danger of over-correction**: Autistic individuals who learn to mask their natural communication style to pass as neurotypical often experience severe mental health consequences. AI social skills training that optimizes purely for "looking normal" could exacerbate this harmful dynamic.

**Whose social world?** Social environments are built by and for neurotypical people—but many of these social norms serve genuine coordination functions, not just neurotypical preferences. The question of which social norms are worth learning and which are arbitrary exclusionary norms that neurotypicals impose on neurodivergent people is a value question, not a technical one.

**Informed consent and cognitive liberty**: If an AI system is designed to make an autistic person more "acceptable" to neurotypicals, has the autistic person consented to this goal? Or is the AI subtly serving the interests of the neurotypical people around them?

**Key Frameworks:** Social cognitive theory (Bandura), autism acceptance vs. cure debates, neurodiversity paradigm (Singer), cognitive-behavioral therapy for social anxiety, pragmatic language intervention
**Key Questions:** How should AI social skills training be designed to respect neurodivergent self-determination? What role should autistic individuals play in designing these systems? How do we prevent AI social skills training from becoming a tool for forced normalization? What evidence standards should apply before AI social skills tools are marketed to vulnerable populations?
