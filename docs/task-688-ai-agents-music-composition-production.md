# AI Agents in Music Composition & Production

The intersection of AI and music—long considered the most human and emotionally irreducible of art forms—is producing profound changes in how music is created, distributed, and consumed. AI music generation has advanced to the point where distinguishing AI-generated from human-composed music is increasingly difficult, raising fundamental questions about creativity, authorship, and the future of professional musicianship.

## AI Music Generation Models

The most dramatic development is the emergence of consumer-accessible AI music generation. Suno AI (v4) and Udio enable users to generate full songs—including vocals, instrumentation, and production—with simple text prompts like "an upbeat indie folk song about summer road trips." MusicLM (Google) generates music from text descriptions with unprecedented fidelity, trained on licensed music datasets.

AIVA (Artificial Intelligence Virtual Artist), one of the earliest professional AI music composition tools, has been certified by France's SACEM as a composer in its own right—the first AI to receive such a copyright registration. AIVA has been used to compose film scores, advertising soundtracks, and game music, working alongside human composers as a creative collaborator rather than replacement.

These models work by learning statistical patterns across millions of songs, identifying relationships between musical elements (melody, harmony, rhythm, instrumentation, dynamics) and textual/descriptive context. The resulting music is genuinely novel—these are not mashups or interpolations but generative outputs that have never existed before.

## AI Agents as Co-Writers and Arrangement Assistants

Beyond fully generative music, AI is transforming the production workflow through co-writing tools. LANDR's AI mastering service has mastered over 10 million tracks, providing professional-grade audio mastering to independent artists who previously couldn't afford studio mastering engineers. LANDR's AI learns from millions of professionally mastered tracks to apply appropriate compression, EQ, and stereo enhancement to raw recordings.

BandLab's Melody Mix uses AI to suggest chord progressions, arrangement structures, and instrumental layering based on the user's existing musical ideas. The tool doesn't generate the creative core—it extends and develops what the human musician has started. This collaborative model, where AI handles the technical optimization and the human provides the creative vision, may represent the most sustainable human-AI creative partnership.

The emerging category of AI "arrangement agents" is particularly valuable for independent musicians. These agents take a basic song structure (perhaps just vocals and guitar) and generate full arrangements with drums, bass, strings, and synths in a specified style. Boomy's one-button song generation generates complete songs with arrangements, while Amadeus Code generates song structures in the style of specific artists.

## AI in Mixing, Mastering, and Audio Engineering

Professional mixing and mastering have historically required expensive studio equipment and years of expertise. AI is democratizing studio-quality sound. iZotope's RX suite uses AI for audio repair—removing hiss, noise, distortion, and unwanted room ambience—with accuracy that surpasses manual editing. Ozone's AI mastering tool analyzes reference tracks and applies appropriate mastering to uploaded audio, competing with human mastering engineers for routine material.

Sonible's smart:eq uses AI to analyze frequency spectrum and listener fatigue curves, automatically applying EQ that maintains tonal balance while reducing fatigue-inducing frequencies. The AI doesn't replace the creative decisions of a mixing engineer—it handles the technical optimization that a good engineer would spend time on manually.

## Style Transfer and "In the Style of" Generation

AI style transfer in music—generating new music in the style of existing artists—raises the most contentious copyright questions in the AI music space. Udio and Suno explicitly enable "in the style of" generation, which critics argue is a form of algorithmic plagiarism. The legal question of whether training an AI model on copyrighted music constitutes infringement, and whether outputting music "in the style of" a specific artist constitutes derivative work, is currently being litigated in multiple jurisdictions.

The music industry is divided. Some artists—particularly independent musicians with smaller audiences—embrace AI generation as a way to produce content at scale. Others, particularly session musicians and session vocalists whose livelihoods depend on being hired for their distinctive sound, view AI style transfer as an existential threat. The Grammy Awards' decision to exclude fully AI-generated works from consideration has set a boundary, but the technology continues to advance faster than regulatory frameworks can adapt.

## Copyright and Authorship Questions

The fundamental legal question—can AI be an author?—has received conflicting answers. The US Copyright Office has consistently held that copyright protection requires human authorship, issuing refusals for AI-generated images and currently reviewing AI-generated music. In 2023, Thaler v. Vidal (the AI "DABUS" case) reached the Supreme Court, which upheld the Copyright Office's position that AI cannot be an inventor or author.

However, the situation is more complex when AI is used as a tool in a creative process that involves significant human direction. A human who uses Suno to generate 100 songs and selects, edits, and sequences the best one may have sufficient creative input to claim authorship. The "sufficient human creativity" standard is emerging as the key test, though it has not been formally adjudicated for music.

## Professional Musicians and AI Tool Use

Interestingly, professional musicians are among the most enthusiastic early adopters of AI tools—but in a collaborative capacity. Composer and producer Richard Devine uses AI generation as a starting point for exploration, then radically transforms the outputs. Hans Zimmer uses AI voice synthesis for demoing film score ideas before presenting them to orchestras. The professional use case is less "AI replaces composer" and more "AI accelerates ideation."

Sync licensing for film, TV, and advertising is being transformed by AI. Tracklib's AI enables producers to create "inspired by" tracks for sync licensing that are guaranteed to be royalty-free and cleared, solving a major friction point in sync licensing.

## Key Companies and the Current Landscape

Adobe's Project Music initiative is developing a comprehensive AI music creation environment within Adobe's creative suite. Amadeus Code (Tokyo) pioneered the AI co-writing model for songwriters. Soundraw (Tokyo) offers AI-generated royalty-free music for content creators. AIVA (Luxembourg) is the leading AI tool for film/game scoring. LANDR (Montreal) dominates AI mastering. Boomy (Austin) enables anyone to generate and distribute AI music to streaming platforms.

The music industry is navigating a genuine paradigm shift. AI will not eliminate music—it may, paradoxically, lead to a golden age of human music-making by freeing musicians from production constraints. The musicians who thrive will be those who use AI as an extension of their creative vision, not those who are replaced by it.
