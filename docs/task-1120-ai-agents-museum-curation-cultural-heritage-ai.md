# AI Agents in Museum Curation & Cultural Heritage AI

## Preserving the Past, Understanding the Present

Museums are fundamentally about meaning-making: selecting which objects to preserve, contextualizing them for audiences, and constructing narratives that help people understand cultural heritage. AI agents are now deeply involved in all aspects of this work, from identifying and authenticating artifacts to generating interpretive content and enabling new forms of audience engagement. The implications for cultural preservation and public understanding of history are profound.

**Identification, Authentication, and Preservation**

One of the most valuable applications of AI in cultural heritage is in the identification and authentication of artifacts. AI systems trained on large datasets of verified objects can identify likely provenance, detect anachronistic materials or techniques, and flag items that warrant further expert examination. This capability is particularly valuable for identifying looted or stolen artifacts and helping institutions understand the history of their collections.

AI also plays an increasingly important role in preservation. Computer vision systems can monitor the condition of artifacts in storage and on display, detecting subtle changes that might indicate deterioration before they become visible to human observers. Some AI systems can predict how artifacts will age under different environmental conditions, helping museums optimize storage and display conditions.

**Interpretive AI and Narrative Generation**

AI agents are transforming how museums generate interpretive content. Rather than relying solely on human scholars to write wall labels, catalogue entries, and educational materials, AI can generate draft interpretive content that scholars then review and refine. This dramatically accelerates the production of educational materials and allows institutions to provide richer context for larger portions of their collections.

Some AI systems can generate personalized interpretive experiences, adapting the depth and framing of content to different audience segments. A visitor interested in technical aspects of ceramics gets different content than one interested in the social context in which those ceramics were used—without requiring human staff to write every possible version.

**Reconstructing the Lost and Damaged**

AI reconstruction is making it possible to experience artifacts and structures that no longer exist in their original form. Damaged manuscripts can be analyzed and reconstructed, demolished buildings can be virtually rebuilt, and fragmented collections can be virtually reassembled. These reconstructions are always interpretive—they involve educated speculation about what is missing—but they make cultural heritage more accessible and comprehensible to public audiences.

**Bias and Representational Justice**

AI curation raises important questions about whose stories get told. Training data for cultural heritage AI inevitably reflects the biases of which collections have been digitized, which objects have been studied, and which narratives have been historically privileged. AI systems may inadvertently amplify existing biases if not carefully designed and monitored.

The most thoughtful museum applications of AI actively work to counteract these biases, using AI to surface underrepresented objects and narratives rather than simply automating the existing canon. Some institutions are using AI to identify gaps in their collections and prioritize acquisitions that would provide more balanced representation. This represents AI as a tool for cultural justice rather than mere efficiency.

**The Human Curation Function**

Despite AI's growing capabilities, the fundamentally ethical and political work of curation—what stories to tell, whose voices to center, how to handle contested histories—remains human. AI can identify patterns, generate options, and process information at scale, but the ultimate decisions about cultural meaning require human judgment, community input, and ethical deliberation. The most productive approach treats AI as a powerful tool that extends the reach of human curators while preserving human agency over the most consequential interpretive decisions.
