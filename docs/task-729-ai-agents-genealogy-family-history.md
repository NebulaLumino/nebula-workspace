# AI Agents in Genealogy & Family History Research

Genealogy is a unique blend of detective work, historical research, and emotional archaeology. The challenge is almost entirely an information problem: family history is fragmented across billions of records, in dozens of languages, in archives that have never been digitized. AI agents are beginning to solve this problem at scale.

## Historical Record Matching and OCR

The foundational challenge in genealogy is taking a handwritten 19th-century census record—written in period-specific handwriting, on degraded paper, with abbreviated names—and connecting it to a specific person in a family tree. This has traditionally required years of expertise.

AI agents now use advanced OCR (optical character recognition) combined with probabilistic name matching to connect records that human transcribers would miss. They handle: variant spellings (John/Jon/Jonathan), transcription errors, phonetic matches, and patronymic naming conventions specific to different cultures and time periods.

Platforms like Ancestry, MyHeritage, and FamilySearch have invested heavily in these systems. MyHeritage's AI Photo Enhancer and DeepQuest features use computer vision and NLP to reconstruct historical photographs and extract information from documents at speeds no human team could match.

## DNA Analysis and AI Matching

DNA genealogy is one of the most successful applications of AI in any domain. When you submit a DNA sample, AI agents compare your genetic markers against millions of other samples to identify genetic relatives—cousins at various degrees of separation—and to infer ancestral origins.

The agent's job is not just matching DNA segments but interpreting what those segments mean: shared DNA segments indicate common ancestors, and the size and location of those segments allow agents to estimate how recently those ancestors lived and in which geographic regions.

FamilyTreeDNA and 23andMe have built increasingly sophisticated AI models that can infer ethnicity estimates, detect recent ancestry connections, and even suggest specific family lines to investigate. These models improve continuously as more samples are added to their databases.

The accuracy of ethnicity estimates deserves some skepticism: they represent probabilistic models, not certain facts. An agent saying "you are 12% Scandinavian" means something specific about the model's training data and assumptions—not that a definitive genealogical fact has been established.

## Building Family Trees from Fragmented Records

The hardest genealogical problem is reconstructing a family tree from records that are incomplete, contradictory, and spread across dozens of archives. AI agents are beginning to approach this as a probabilistic inference problem rather than a deterministic search.

An agent can take: a census record showing a family in 1850, a marriage record from 1847, a land deed from 1860, and a military pension record from 1890—and probabilistically link them, identify likely relationships, and build a family tree even when no single record contains complete information.

The agent also identifies contradictions that require human investigation: records that seem to describe the same person but with conflicting details. This frees human genealogists to do the interpretive work that requires human judgment while automating the information retrieval and preliminary analysis.

## Ethical Considerations

DNA genealogy raises significant ethical questions. A DNA test taken by one family member reveals genetic information about relatives who didn't consent to testing. AI matching can discover non-paternity events, unknown siblings, and close relatives that people never knew existed—information that can be emotionally devastating.

Responsible genealogy AI platforms are developing norms around consent and disclosure, but the technology has moved faster than the ethics. A non-consenting relative's genetic information is now, in some sense, in the database—and once it's there, it's hard to remove.
