# AI Agents in Carbon Capture Economics & Direct Air Capture

## Overview

Carbon capture — both point-source capture from industrial facilities and direct air capture (DAC) that pulls CO2 directly from the atmosphere — is increasingly central to most climate stabilization scenarios. The IPCC's 1.5°C pathways require removing 100-1000 gigatons of CO2 from the atmosphere by 2100, a removal scale that is economically and logistically without historical precedent. Current DAC costs range from $250-$600 per ton of CO2, compared to the $50-$100 price range that most analysts consider necessary for meaningful scale. AI agents are now being deployed across the carbon capture value chain — from materials science research to economic optimization of capture processes and market design for carbon removal credits.

## How AI Agents Are Applied

**DAC Materials & Process Optimization:** AI agents are dramatically accelerating the discovery of better sorbent and solvent materials for DAC. Traditional materials science involves slow, expensive laboratory trial and error. AI agents can screen millions of candidate materials computationally, predicting adsorption capacity, selectivity, regeneration energy requirements, and degradation rates — guiding experimentalists to the most promising candidates. Agents have already helped identify new MOF (metal-organic framework) materials and amine-functionalized sorbents with significantly improved performance. Beyond materials, agents optimize the thermodynamic cycle of DAC plants — balancing capture efficiency, energy input, and regeneration conditions.

**Plant Design & Scaling Optimization:** Building the first generation of commercial DAC plants requires navigating enormous uncertainty about optimal plant designs, locations, and operating conditions. AI agents model different plant configurations (liquid solvent vs. solid sorbent DAC), geographic siting strategies (co-located with renewable energy, industrial point sources, or distributed), and operational parameters. Agents can run thousands of design scenarios to find configurations that minimize levelized cost of capture (LCCO2).

**Carbon Accounting & MRV (Measurement, Reporting, Verification):** The credibility of carbon markets depends on robust verification that captured CO2 was actually removed from the atmosphere and durably stored. AI agents continuously monitor DAC plant operations, satellite data showing injection well performance, and geochemical sensors tracking stored CO2 to provide tamper-resistant, auditable verification of carbon removal. Agents can detect anomalies in plant operations that might indicate measurement errors or fraudulent claims.

**Carbon Market Design & Trading:** AI agents are becoming central to the emerging voluntary carbon removal market. They match buyers (corporations with net-zero commitments) with sellers (DAC operators, enhanced weathering projects, biochar producers), price carbon removal credits based on cost curves and demand projections, and structure long-term removal agreements that provide the revenue certainty needed to justify DAC investment. Agents can also model the macroeconomic implications of large-scale DAC deployment — Who pays? How are costs distributed? What are the distributional effects?

**Integration with Carbon Utilization:** Captured CO2 can be used in a variety of ways — as a feedstock for synthetic fuels, building materials, chemicals, and even food and beverage applications. AI agents model utilization pathways, identifying markets for captured CO2 that can improve the economics of DAC while displacing virgin carbon emissions. Agents optimize the supply chain between capture facilities and utilization sites.

**Life Cycle Assessment (LCA):** Any honest accounting of DAC must include the emissions associated with building and operating the plant, including the energy inputs required for sorbent regeneration and CO2 compression. AI agents perform continuous LCA, tracking the full carbon balance of each DAC facility — from steel and concrete in construction through operational energy use to long-term storage verification. This provides the transparency that carbon markets require to function honestly.

## Key Technologies & Datasets

Materials informatics uses density functional theory (DFT) calculations and machine learning force fields to predict material properties computationally. Process systems engineering models (Aspen Plus, gPROMS) feed agent optimization engines for plant design. Earth observation and subsurface modeling assess geological storage permanence. Integration with energy system models (PLEXOS, TIMES) allows agents to analyze DAC within the broader energy transition context.

## Challenges & Considerations

The economics of DAC remain deeply challenging — at $250-600/ton, it's still far too expensive for mass deployment without massive policy subsidies. AI optimization can improve efficiency and reduce costs, but whether it can drive costs low enough fast enough is an open question. There's also a moral hazard concern: the promise of future DAC could reduce urgency for immediate emissions reductions. AI agents that model both carbon capture and emissions reduction pathways are uniquely positioned to quantify this trade-off.

## Outlook

The most transformative near-term application is AI-accelerated materials discovery — finding sorbents that can reduce DAC energy requirements by 50% or more, which would dramatically improve economics. In the longer term, AI agents that can manage integrated "carbon factories" — DAC plants coupled with renewable energy, CO2 storage, and utilization facilities — as autonomous operations would represent a fundamental shift in how carbon removal is delivered. The technology is nascent but advancing rapidly.
