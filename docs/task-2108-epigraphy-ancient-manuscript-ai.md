# AI in Epigraphy & Ancient Manuscript Analysis

Epigraphy — the study of inscriptions carved, painted, or impressed on durable materials — is one of the oldest branches of archaeology, providing direct testimony from ancient societies in their own words. From Roman tombstones to Mayan glyphs, from cuneiform tablets to medieval charters, inscriptions have shaped our understanding of history. But millions of inscriptions remain unstudied, their scripts undeciphered, their texts unread. Artificial intelligence is now accelerating every stage of epigraphic work, from field discovery to scholarly interpretation.

## Automated Reading of Damaged and Degraded Inscriptions

One of the greatest challenges in epigraphy is that inscriptions are almost never pristine. Stone erodes, metal corrodes, pottery shards break, and centuries of burial destroy or obscure text. Scholars spend years training to read damaged inscriptions, building mental models of letterforms and context to fill in gaps. AI is now matching and exceeding human performance at this task.

Computer vision models trained on large datasets of inscriptions can now read letterforms even when significant portions are missing, using context — surrounding letters, word patterns, known phrases — to propose reconstructions. For Greek and Latin inscriptions, AI systems trained on the massive corpus of already-read texts can suggest completions for damaged words with impressive accuracy. For undeciphered scripts like Etruscan or Meroitic, neural models are helping to identify structural patterns that may eventually support decipherment.

## 3D Reconstruction and Light-Weight Modeling

Ancient inscriptions are inherently three-dimensional objects — carved in stone, stamped in clay, incised in metal. Recent advances in photogrammetry and structured-light 3D scanning have made it possible to create detailed 3D models of inscriptions from photographs. AI enhances these models by automatically extracting the inscribed surface, modeling the depth and angle of cuts, and rendering the text under simulated lighting conditions that reveal marks invisible to the naked eye.

Relief and counter-relief inscriptions — where letters stand out from or recede into the surface — are particularly amenable to 3D analysis. By modeling how light would fall across a stone surface at different angles, AI can reconstruct the reading conditions that the original carvers intended, helping epigraphists understand why certain letters were carved in specific ways.

## Handwriting Analysis and Scribal Identification

Beyond formal inscriptions, ancient manuscripts — papyri, codices, wax tablets — preserve the handwriting of individual scribes. Identifying scribal hands can reveal how texts were produced, who wrote them, where they were written, and sometimes who owned them. AI-based handwriting analysis is now being applied to ancient corpora with significant results.

For Egyptian papyri, machine learning models trained on well-documented scribal hands can identify the probable scribe of a newly discovered text with reasonable accuracy, grouping texts by scribal workshop or geographical origin. For medieval manuscripts, similar techniques can distinguish the hands of different scribes copying the same text, revealing the division of labor in medieval scriptoria.

## Text Reconstruction and Textual Criticism

Classical scholars have long used textual criticism — the analysis of differences between copies of the same text — to reconstruct the original form of ancient works. This is particularly important for texts like the Homeric epics or the Bible, which survive in hundreds of manuscript copies with thousands of minor differences. AI is now automating the comparison of manuscript traditions, identifying variant readings, and proposing original reconstructions based on patterns invisible to human readers.

Stemmatics — the attempt to create a family tree of manuscripts based on shared errors — has traditionally relied on manual analysis of hundreds or thousands of variant readings. Neural networks and transformer models can now process entire manuscript traditions at once, proposing stemma that account for complex patterns of borrowing and revision. When combined with paleographic dating of the manuscripts themselves, these AI-generated stemma can help date the original composition more precisely.

## AI and the Digital Humanities

Perhaps the most transformative application of AI in epigraphy is the creation of large, searchable databases of ancient texts. Projects like the Corpus Inscriptionum Latinarum, Packard Humanities Institute, and Inscriptiones Graecae have been digitizing inscriptions for decades, but AI is now enabling full-text search, linguistic analysis, and cross-referencing at a scale impossible for human researchers. Named entity recognition, part-of-speech tagging, and semantic analysis of ancient languages — all powered by transformer models fine-tuned on classical corpora — are opening new avenues of research that span thousands of texts.
