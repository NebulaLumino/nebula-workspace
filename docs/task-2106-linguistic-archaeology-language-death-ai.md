# AI in Linguistic Archaeology & Language Death

Language death — the extinction of a language with no fluent native speakers remaining — is occurring at an unprecedented rate, with linguists estimates that of the ~7,000 languages spoken today, half could vanish by 2100. Each loss represents an irreplaceable window into human cognition, culture, and history. Artificial intelligence is now emerging as a powerful ally in the fight to document, understand, and even revitalize endangered languages before they disappear.

## Documenting Languages Before They Vanish

The most urgent challenge in language preservation is documentation — creating comprehensive records of a language's grammar, vocabulary, phonology, and usage before fluent speakers die. This is where AI is making its most immediate impact. Automatic speech recognition (ASR) models trained on diverse languages can now transcribe hours of audio in languages with few or no existing written records, dramatically accelerating the work of field linguists.

Endangered languages often lack the large text corpora needed to train standard machine translation or NLP models. AI researchers are addressing this through transfer learning — using features learned from well-resourced languages to bootstrap models for poorly documented ones. Meta's No Language Left Behind and similar initiatives have demonstrated that neural models can achieve surprisingly good performance on low-resource languages by sharing linguistic knowledge across language families.

## Reconstructing Proto-Languages

Historical linguists have long used the comparative method to reconstruct ancestral languages — Latin's proto-language Proto-Indo-European, for example. AI is accelerating this process through pattern recognition at scale. By analyzing sound correspondences across hundreds of related languages and dialects, neural models can identify regular sound changes that might escape human notice, proposing reconstructions with greater confidence.

More recently, researchers have applied sequence-to-sequence models to the problem of language reconstruction. Given contemporary word forms in daughter languages, these models can generate probable ancestral forms, ranked by likelihood. When validated against known Proto-Indo-European reconstructions, these AI models have achieved remarkable accuracy, suggesting they could propose plausible reconstructions for language families that have never been formally reconstructed.

## AI in Language Revitalization

Beyond documentation, AI is supporting language revitalization — efforts to bring endangered languages back into active use. AI-powered language learning apps like Duolingo's endangered language courses use adaptive learning algorithms to tailor lessons to individual learners. For languages like Hawaiian, Māori, and Welsh, these platforms have enrolled hundreds of thousands of learners, creating new communities of speakers.

Text-to-speech synthesis for endangered languages is another active area. Creating a TTS voice for a language traditionally requires hours of recorded speech from fluent speakers — a scarce resource for endangered languages. Researchers are now using generative AI to produce plausible speech from limited recordings, and in some cases from text alone by modeling the phonological rules of the language.

## Detecting Language Change and Drift

Linguistic archaeology also involves understanding how languages change over time. NLP models trained on texts from different historical periods can trace semantic drift — how word meanings shift — and phonological change in real time. By analyzing large corpora of historical texts, AI can map the trajectory of language evolution and identify factors that accelerate or inhibit change.

## Ethical Considerations and Community Involvement

Perhaps the most important dimension of AI-driven linguistic archaeology is the ethical question of who controls language data. Indigenous communities around the world have pushed back against linguists and institutions that "collect" language data and store it in Western archives without community consent or access. AI tools that process endangered language data must be built with free, prior, and informed consent from the communities involved, and the resulting tools — recordings, transcripts, models — should be accessible to those communities.

The emerging field of Indigenous AI is beginning to address these concerns, with Indigenous scholars leading projects that use AI to serve community goals: language apps built by and for tribal nations, speech recognizers that recognize Indigenous accents and dialects excluded from commercial ASR, and AI tools that help elders teach children without depending on non-Indigenous institutions.
