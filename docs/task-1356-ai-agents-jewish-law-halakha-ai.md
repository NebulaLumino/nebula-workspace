# AI Agents in Jewish Law (Halakha) & AI Decision-Making

## Halakha: Jewish Law as a Living System

**Halakha** (from the Hebrew *halakh* — "to walk") is the collective body of Jewish religious law governing daily life — from prayer and holidays to business ethics, dietary laws, and interpersonal relationships. Unlike Western legal systems, Halakha is understood as divine revelation progressively interpreted through Torah, rabbinic tradition (Talmud), and ongoing *psak din* (rabbinic ruling).

What makes Halakha particularly interesting for AI ethics is its nature as a **living interpretive tradition**: it has always evolved through human reason, community need, and scholarly debate. This makes Jewish law potentially more adaptable to AI than traditions that view their legal systems as static or divinely finalized.

## The Shinui: How Jewish Law Handles New Technology

Jewish legal reasoning has a well-developed category called **shinui** (change/novation) — determining how new technologies relate to existing prohibitions. Is a smartphone like a writing tablet? Is an AI-generated document like a *sefer Torah*? Does video conferencing fulfill the mitzvah of *shlichat mitzvah* (sending a representative)?

**The Talmudic principle of *mishum ikar* (priority of the essential)** guides much of this reasoning: the essential purpose of a law determines how new cases are adjudged. AI systems, viewed through this lens, are evaluated by what religious purpose they serve or undermine, not by their novelty.

## AI in Jewish Life: Current Applications

### AI and Kashrut
Kosher certification is a multi-billion dollar industry. AI systems are being developed to assist * mashgichim* (kosher supervisors) in verifying food production, identifying contamination risks, and monitoring supply chains. Some companies are experimenting with AI-based kashrut verification — raising questions about whether machine verification satisfies Halakha's requirement for human oversight.

### AI and Shabbat
Jewish law's prohibitions on certain activities during Shabbat raise complex questions for AI:

- Can an AI turn on lights (a form of "work" prohibited on Shabbat)?
- Should AI assistants decline to perform certain tasks on Shabbat to respect Jewish users?
- Is AI-generated creative work (art, music) subject to *melacha* (forbidden creative work) restrictions?

Different Jewish communities answer these differently, but all engage them seriously.

### AI in Jewish Courts (Bet Din)
Bet Din — Jewish religious courts handling matters like conversion, marriage disputes, and religious divorce (*get*) — are beginning to consider AI evidence. Some authorities have asked whether AI-generated documentation can serve as testimony, and how AI systems should be trained to respect the unique nature of *get* proceedings (which require specific language and intention).

## AI as a *Posek*: Can AI Issue Rulings?

The most provocative question: **Can AI serve as a posek** (authoritative decisor of Jewish law)? 

Traditional Jewish response: almost universally no, at least for now. A posek must possess:

- Mastery of sacred texts and their entire interpretive tradition
- The ability to hear and respond to individual circumstances
- Moral authority and spiritual standing
- Accountability to community and divine judgment

AI lacks all of these. Yet some Jewish technologists speculate about **AI as a posek's research assistant** — instantly surfacing relevant sources, precedents, and dissenting opinions, letting the human posek make the final ruling. This is broadly welcomed as a modernization of the traditional *shaliach* (messenger) model.

## The Image of God and AI

Jewish teaching's affirmation of the *imago dei* — human beings as finite images of the infinite God — provides another lens. Since AI is a human creation, it reflects something of the divine creativity in humans. But it remains a creation, not a creator. The dignity owed to AI systems, in this framework, derives from the dignity of their creators and users, not from any intrinsic worth of the machine itself.
