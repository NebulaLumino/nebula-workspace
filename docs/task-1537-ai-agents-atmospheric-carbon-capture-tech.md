# AI Agents in Atmospheric Carbon Capture Technology

As the world struggles to reduce emissions fast enough to limit warming to 1.5°C, attention is increasingly turning to carbon dioxide removal (CDR)—actively extracting CO2 from the atmosphere. Even under the most optimistic emissions reduction scenarios, IPCC models consistently require billions of tons of annual CDR by mid-century to achieve net-negative emissions. AI is playing an increasingly central role in the development, optimization, and deployment of atmospheric carbon capture technologies.

## The CDR Landscape

Carbon dioxide removal approaches span an enormous range of technological maturity, cost, and scalability:

- **Nature-based solutions**: Reforestation, wetland restoration, soil carbon sequestration, blue carbon (mangroves, seagrass)
- **Technology-enhanced nature**: Bioenergy with carbon capture and storage (BECCS), enhanced weathering, ocean alkalinity enhancement
- **Technological CDR**: Direct air capture (DAC), ocean-based CDR, biochar, accelerated silicate weathering

Each approach carries different cost structures, permanence guarantees, co-benefits, and risks. AI agents are being applied across this entire spectrum.

## Direct Air Capture Optimization

Direct air capture (DAC) facilities pull ambient air through chemical sorbents or solid materials that bind CO2, which is then released through heating and collected for permanent storage. Current DAC plants cost $400–600 per ton of CO2 to operate, though costs are falling rapidly with technological learning.

AI agents optimize DAC operations across multiple parameters simultaneously: sorbent regeneration temperature curves, fan speed optimization to balance CO2 uptake rates against energy costs, predictive maintenance for sorbent materials and rotating equipment, and integration with renewable energy sources to minimize carbon intensity of the capture process.

Most ambitiously, AI-accelerated materials discovery is targeting the development of next-generation sorbents with superior CO2 binding kinetics, lower regeneration energy requirements, and longer operational lifetimes. High-throughput computational screening of candidate materials—calculating CO2 binding energies for millions of hypothetical metal-organic frameworks (MOFs)—is being guided by machine learning surrogate models that predict promising candidates from cheap descriptors rather than expensive quantum chemical calculations.

## Ocean-Based CDR

The ocean contains approximately 50 times more carbon than the atmosphere and already absorbs ~25% of annual anthropogenic CO2 emissions. Ocean-based CDR approaches seek to accelerate this natural process.

AI agents are being developed to optimize ocean alkalinity enhancement—adding alkaline minerals (like olivine) to seawater to increase its carbon storage capacity. The challenge is vast: to meaningfully impact atmospheric CO2 would require processing billions of tons of silicate minerals. AI models optimize mineral grinding size for dissolution rates, predict dispersion and mixing in ocean currents, and model impacts on marine carbonate chemistry across different ocean basins.

Similarly, for ocean iron fertilization—adding iron to iron-limited HNLC (high-nutrient, low-chlorophyll) ocean regions to stimulate phytoplankton growth that sequesters carbon—AI agents optimize fertilization strategies based on real-time oceanographic modeling, minimizing waste and unintended ecological impacts.

## Monitoring, Reporting, and Verification (MRV)

The credibility of CDR as a climate strategy depends entirely on trustworthy monitoring, reporting, and verification (MRV). For technology CDR like DAC, AI enables continuous, automated measurement of CO2 capture rates, purity, and storage permanence. For nature-based CDR, AI combines satellite imagery (Sentinel, GEDI lidar), ground sensor networks, and ecosystem models to estimate carbon sequestration with quantified uncertainty.

The emergence of carbon credit markets creates strong incentives for AI-driven MRV systems that can detect fraudulent carbon credit claims—overestimated sequestration, impermanence violations, leakage. AI agents that continuously audit carbon project data streams against physical constraints, detect statistical anomalies, and flag high-risk projects for human review are becoming standard infrastructure for carbon registries.

## AI-Enabled Climate Intervention at Scale

Perhaps the most consequential—and contested—AI application in CDR is the design and optimization of solar geoengineering or carbon removal at climate-altering scales. Some researchers contemplate deliberate interventions—stratospheric aerosol injection, marine cloud brightening—that would involve intentionally modifying Earth's radiative balance. AI models are essential for evaluating these interventions: predicting regional climate effects, identifying dangerous side effects, and optimizing intervention parameters.

These applications blur the line between optimization and governance. An AI agent making real-time decisions about stratospheric aerosol injection is not merely a technical system but an adjudicating authority over the planet's climate. Who controls that agent, what objectives it optimizes, and what safeguards prevent catastrophic error are questions that cannot be answered by technical means alone.

The CDR field faces a critical timing challenge: technologies that take 20 years to deploy at scale are needed now. AI-accelerated learning—by optimizing existing CDR systems, shortening R&D cycles, and reducing deployment risk—is one of the most promising paths to bridging this gap. The question is whether CDR will be deployed at sufficient scale, with sufficient rigor, to meet the moment that climate science demands.
