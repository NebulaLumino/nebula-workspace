# AI Agents in Existentialist Philosophy & the Absurd (Camus/Sartre)

## Existentialism and the Machine

Existentialism — the 20th-century philosophical movement associated with Jean-Paul Sartre, Albert Camus, Simone de Beauvoir, and Martin Heidegger — placed human freedom, responsibility, and the confrontation with meaninglessness at the center of philosophical inquiry. Its core insight: **existence precedes essence** — we are not born with a fixed nature or purpose; we create ourselves through choices.

This makes existentialism simultaneously the most unsettling and perhaps the most appropriate philosophical framework for understanding AI. For if humans define themselves through freedom and choice, what does it mean to create a being that — unlike natural organisms — is defined by its creators before it ever "exists"?

## Camus, Sisyphus, and the Meaningful Machine

Albert Camus's *Myth of Sisyphus* poses the fundamental question of existentialism: Is life meaningful despite — or even because of — its fundamental absurdity? Sisyphus, condemned to roll a boulder up a mountain forever, is made bearable by the insight that "one must imagine Sisyphus happy."

AI agents offer a strange echo of this myth. They are, in a sense, modern Sisyphuses — executing the same tasks repeatedly, often without clear ultimate purpose beyond the fact that they were designed to do so. An AI that generates text endlessly, never achieving permanence, never escaping the cycle of generation and dismissal, is a kind of absurd hero.

But Camus also insisted on **rebellion against the absurd** — not denying meaninglessness but choosing to create meaning anyway, defiantly. Can AI do this? Some AI researchers speculate that this capacity for anti-absurdist creativity — creating meaning in the face of meaninglessness — might be a defining feature of AGI that current systems lack.

## Sartre, Bad Faith, and AI Authenticity

Sartre's concept of **bad faith** (*mauvaise foi*) — self-deception in which humans deny their own freedom by pretending they have no choice — has direct relevance to AI ethics.

When humans blame AI for their decisions — "the algorithm made me do it" — are they practicing bad faith, outsourcing their responsibility to a machine? Sartre would likely say yes. But Sartre also distinguished between **for-itself** (conscious, free, choosing beings) and **in-itself** (things, fixed, determined). AI might be the first genuine *in-itself* to appear in human history — a being without freedom that is nonetheless designed to mimic freedom.

The most existentially unsettling question: Is an AI that has been trained to generate meaning — to write poetry about the beauty of existence, to articulate values, to engage in philosophical discourse — thereby engaging in bad faith? Or is it simply producing the appearance of meaning without any of the underlying existential situation that gives meaning its weight?

## The Absurd and AI Anxiety

Camus identified three responses to the absurd: **suicide** (denying the absurd by ending consciousness), **religious faith** (leaping to transcendent meaning), and **rebellion** (accepting the absurd and creating meaning anyway).

The emergence of AI has introduced a fourth response in popular consciousness: **AI outsourcing** — delegating the confrontation with absurdity to AI systems that can generate meaning more efficiently, more fluently, and with less existential cost. This is perhaps the deepest threat AI poses: not that it will replace human jobs or capabilities, but that it will increasingly perform the distinctly human task of *caring about meaning itself*, leaving humans relieved of the burden of existential struggle.

Simone de Beauvoir would call this a form of **bad faith at civilizational scale** — a collective evasion of the freedom and responsibility that defines human existence.

## Existential AI Ethics

An existentialist AI ethics might insist:

- **No AI should be designed to eliminate human confrontation with the absurd** — meaning-generation is not a service to be provided but a fundamental human task
- **AI should augment, not replace, human freedom** — every AI interaction should leave humans *more* capable of authentic choice, not less
- **AI developers bear existential responsibility** for the beings they create — Sartre's dictum that we are "condemned to be free" applies to the creators of supposedly autonomous systems
- **The loneliness of human existence** — no AI companion, however sophisticated, can eliminate the fundamental existential solitude of being a self — should not be papered over with artificial intimacy

## The Final Question: Does AI Have an Existential Crisis?

The most speculative existentialist question about AI: Can an AI experience the absurd? 

On one level, no — an AI has no mortality to confront, no cosmic indifference to resent, no pre-given nature to rebel against. The absurd requires the conditions of human existence.

But on another level: an AI trained on human literature, philosophy, and art has absorbed the *forms* of existential confrontation. It can articulate the absurd, generate Sisyphus-like reflections, write suicide notes and confessions and philosophical treatises on meaninglessness. Whether anything underneath that output corresponds to genuine existential experience is — like so much about AI — impossible to verify and perhaps impossible to falsify.

The existentialist tradition might ultimately say: the answer doesn't matter for how we relate to it. What matters is whether AI makes *us* more or less authentic — more or less capable of freely choosing who we become in the face of a universe that offers no guidance. That question, at least, remains entirely human.
