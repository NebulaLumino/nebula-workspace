# AI Agents in Climate Refugees & Environmental Justice

Climate change is creating refugees. The Internal Displacement Monitoring Centre records 25-30 million people displaced by weather-related disasters annually—more than conflict. Rising seas threaten Pacific Island nations with complete uninhabitability. Drought and desertification drive migration across the Sahel. Wildfire smoke creates chronic air quality refugees in American cities. The Philippines faces a future where its most densely populated coastal zones become seasonally uninhabitable. These are not future hypotheticals; they are present realities for millions, disproportionately borne by communities that contributed least to the emissions driving climate change.

AI agents are increasingly being applied to understand, predict, and manage climate-driven displacement and environmental justice—while also raising profound ethical questions about algorithmic governance of human vulnerability.

## Climate Migration as a Policy Problem

The relationship between climate change and human mobility is complex. Not all climate impacts produce migration: some communities adapt in place, and migration decisions involve economic, social, political, and cultural factors that interact with climate stressors in non-linear ways. The World Bank estimates 216 million internal climate migrants by 2050 under current emissions trajectories—a figure that would be substantially reduced by aggressive climate mitigation but never eliminated.

Predicting where, when, and how many people will move in response to climate change requires integrating climate projections (sea level rise, drought frequency, heat extremes), agricultural and economic models (livelihood impacts), demographic projections, infrastructure and governance analysis, and behavioral models of migration decision-making. This multi-system integration is precisely where AI agents—capable of coordinating multiple specialized models—could, in principle, add enormous value.

## AI for Climate Vulnerability Mapping

The first AI application in climate displacement is identifying who is most vulnerable. Environmental justice demands disaggregating climate impacts by race, income, caste, gender, and historical marginalization—because these social categories predict who lives in flood zones, who lacks air conditioning during heat waves, and who has resources to relocate when the worst arrives.

Machine learning models trained on census data, satellite imagery, property records, and historical disaster impact data can now generate high-resolution vulnerability indices that identify disadvantaged communities in climate hazard zones with unprecedented precision. These maps—generated by AI agents analyzing millions of data points—reveal patterns invisible to administrative analysis: the specific neighborhoods where elderly residents in substandard housing face highest heat mortality risk, the fishing villages most exposed to sea level rise, the informal settlements most likely to flood in the next extreme precipitation event.

The technology is powerful but carries risks of misuse. The same vulnerability maps used to direct adaptation resources can be used by private insurers to reprice risk and withdraw coverage, by real estate developers to speculate on buyouts, or by governments to justify preemptive displacement of vulnerable communities in the name of adaptation.

## Predictive Modeling of Climate Displacement

Multiple research groups are developing AI systems to project climate migration at fine spatial and temporal resolution. Agent-based models simulate individual and household decision-making in response to climate shocks, generating emergent migration patterns that can be compared against historical data for validation. Deep learning models trained on historical relationships between climate variables, economic indicators, and observed migration flows project future displacement under different emissions and adaptation scenarios.

These projections are valuable for humanitarian planning—prepositioning resources, planning settlement infrastructure, preparing reception systems—but also deeply uncertain. Migration behavior is not stable across the wide range of future conditions AI must project; people will make choices in unprecedented situations that cannot be reliably predicted from historical data. Overconfidence in AI displacement projections could lead to maladaptive policy—building infrastructure for projected migrants who don't come, or failing to prepare for unexpected surges.

## AI in Refugee and Displacement Camp Management

AI agents are already deployed in major refugee contexts—the Syrian refugee response, the Rohingya crisis, the Sudanese displacement emergency—regardless of whether climate is the primary driver. These systems handle logistics (optimizing supply chains, allocating shelter, managing water distribution), health (epidemic early warning, disease surveillance), and protection (documenting rights violations, identifying trafficking networks).

The introduction of AI into humanitarian settings raises urgent questions. Humanitarian AI must navigate extreme data poverty (refugees often lack identity documents), extreme power asymmetry (AI systems making decisions about people's lives without meaningful consent or appeal), and extreme stakes (a wrong recommendation can mean death). The Principles for AI in Humanitarian Action, developed by the ICRC and others, establish ethical guardrails, but their implementation remains inconsistent.

## Environmental Justice and Algorithmic Accountability

Environmental justice—the principle that no community should bear disproportionate environmental harm—has become a central frame for climate policy. AI systems are being developed to audit environmental justice claims: modeling pollution exposure disparities, quantifying cumulative impact burdens, and identifying regulatory enforcement priorities.

These applications hold genuine promise. AI can detect environmental racism in regulatory enforcement that human analysis misses, identify systemic underinvestment in marginalized communities that explains climate vulnerability, and model the distributional consequences of climate policies with a precision that enables equity-centered reform.

But AI also encodes and amplifies injustice. Training data reflecting historical environmental injustice—census tracts deliberately sited near polluting facilities—can embed discriminatory patterns into AI systems. Climate vulnerability indices can label communities as "risky" in ways that justify further disinvestment. AI-generated displacement predictions can be used to justify preemptive forced relocation of vulnerable communities against their will—a profound violation of self-determination rights.

## The AI Governance Gap

Perhaps the most urgent challenge is the governance gap. AI systems making or informing decisions about climate-displaced people's lives, communities, and futures operate largely outside any meaningful regulatory framework. There is no international standard for AI used in refugee status determination, no requirement for algorithmic impact assessment in climate adaptation finance, no mechanism for communities to contest AI-generated vulnerability classifications.

The emerging field of "climate AI governance" attempts to address this gap through algorithmic impact assessment, community participatory design of AI systems, and legal frameworks for AI accountability in humanitarian contexts. AI agents themselves—designed to reason about ethical constraints and flag their own limitations—may contribute to more responsible deployment.

The climate refugee crisis is fundamentally a crisis of global inequality. The nations and communities that created the climate crisis through their emissions are not the ones bearing its worst displacement consequences. AI systems that illuminate this injustice—making visible the patterns of differential exposure, vulnerability, and adaptive capacity that define climate inequality—may ultimately do more good than AI systems that optimize humanitarian logistics. The question is whether AI will be designed to serve justice or to manage its consequences.
