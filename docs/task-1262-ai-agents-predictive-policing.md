# AI Agents in Predictive Policing & Criminal Justice Reform

## Overview

Predictive policing uses AI algorithms to forecast where crimes are likely to occur or who is likely to commit them. Promoted as a way to allocate police resources more efficiently, these systems have generated intense controversy over bias, accuracy, and their role in perpetuating mass incarceration—particularly of Black and Latino Americans.

## Two Flavors of Predictive Policing

**Place-Based Predictive Policing** — Systems like PredPol (now Geolitica) and HunchLab analyze historical crime data, social conditions, weather, and other variables to generate "risk maps" identifying geographic areas where crime is predicted to spike. Police presence is then concentrated in these areas.

**Person-Based Predictive Policing** — More controversial, these systems score individuals based on arrest records, social network connections, criminal history, and other factors to predict their likelihood of committing or becoming a victim of crime. Examples include Chicago's Strategic Subject List and various "heat list" systems that flag individuals for enhanced surveillance or intervention.

## The Evidence Base Is Shaky

Despite enthusiastic adoption by police departments, the empirical evidence for predictive policing's effectiveness is thin at best. A 2020 RAND Corporation study found that place-based predictive policing systems showed "mixed results" and in some cases performed no better than random patrol allocation. A National Institute of Justice review concluded there was "insufficient evidence" to determine whether these systems reduce crime.

## Bias and the Feedback Loop Problem

The most serious criticism of predictive policing is that it encodes and amplifies historical policing biases. Because these systems train on historical arrest data, and historically police have disproportionately surveilled Black and Latino neighborhoods, the AI learns to predict crime where policing has been concentrated—regardless of actual crime rates.

This creates a self-fulfilling feedback loop: more police in communities of color → more arrests in those communities → AI predicts more crime there → even more police deployed. The algorithm mistakes policing patterns for crime patterns.

**The COMPAS Controversy** — ProPublica's landmark 2016 analysis of the COMPAS recidivism prediction algorithm (used widely in sentencing) found it falsely labeled Black defendants as higher risk at nearly twice the rate of white defendants. Northpointe (now Equivant), the company behind COMPAS, disputed the methodology, but the controversy catalyzed a national conversation about algorithmic bias in criminal justice.

## Person-Based Systems and Minority Report Fantasies

Chicago's Strategic Subject List has been used to monitor individuals identified as at high risk of involvement in gun violence. The list reportedly included people who had never been charged with any crime. Critics argue this constitutes preemptive punishment based on probabilistic guilt—violating fundamental principles of due process.

## Reform-Oriented Applications

Not all AI in criminal justice leans toward harsher enforcement. Reform-minded applications include:

- **Risk Assessment in Bail Decisions** — Algorithms like the Arnold Foundation's PSA aim to inform pretrial detention decisions with actuarial data rather than cash bail, potentially reducing mass pretrial detention.
- **Prosecutorial Screening** — AI tools can flag cases that lack sufficient evidence for prosecution, potentially reducing wrongful convictions.
- **Wrongful Conviction Review** — Pattern recognition AI can analyze trial transcripts and forensic evidence to identify potential wrongful convictions.
- **Sentencing Alternatives** — AI can model which types of interventions (treatment programs, community service, restorative justice) are most effective for different defendant profiles.

## The Path Forward

Several states and cities have moved to restrict predictive policing. California passed the Predictive Policing Prohibition Act in 2020. New York City passed a surveillance transparency law after community pushback.

Criminal justice reformers argue that even well-intentioned AI tools are premature without: (1) independent bias audits, (2) public transparency about how systems work, (3) meaningful appeals mechanisms, and (4) evidence of accuracy and fairness across demographic groups.

The deeper debate is philosophical: is it just to treat people as statistical probabilities? And who bears the cost when algorithms get it wrong?
