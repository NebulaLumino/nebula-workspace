## The Promise of One-on-One Tutoring, Scaled

Every parent knows that a good human tutor can transform a struggling student's relationship with a subject. Studies consistently show that one-on-one tutoring produces learning gains 2 standard deviations above classroom instruction — the "Doron effect." But hiring a great tutor costs $100–$300/hour, putting personalized instruction out of reach for most families.

AI tutoring agents promise to close this gap. Khan Academy's Khanmigo, Carnegie Learning's MATHia, and dozens of adaptive platforms now use large language models to deliver Socratic tutoring conversations at scale. What happens when these systems become sophisticated enough to serve as primary instructors?

## What's Actually Working

### Intelligent Tutoring Systems (ITS)

The predecessors to modern AI agents — rule-based ITS systems like Cognitive tutors — have 30+ years of evidence behind them. Carnegie Learning's AI math tutor has been shown in randomized trials to produce learning gains 1.5–2x above traditional instruction. These systems work by maintaining a precise model of what a student knows and doesn't know, then selecting problems at the edge of their competence.

### Large Language Model Tutors

The breakthrough with LLMs is natural language flexibility. Khanmigo, built on GPT-4, can hold multi-turn conversations about history, science, and writing — something rigid rule-based systems couldn't do. It can explain concepts in different ways until a student signals understanding. It never loses patience.

Early pilots show promise: students using Khanmigo show higher engagement rates and more completed exercises than those using traditional platforms. But the long-term learning outcome data is still being collected.

## What AI Tutors Can't Do (Yet)

### Social-Emotional Learning

Learning is social. A great teacher doesn't just transmit knowledge — they model curiosity, validate struggle, notice when a student is having a bad day, and adapt accordingly. AI tutors can detect frustration patterns in typing speed and response accuracy, but they can't genuinely *care* in the way a human mentor does.

### Motivational Anchoring

Students learn from teachers they admire and want to impress. The social pressure of a respected human observer — "my teacher is watching me work" — has measurable motivational effects that AI tutors currently can't replicate. Dopamine and social presence are intertwined in ways that make purely digital tutoring less sticky.

### Teaching How to Think, Not Just What to Know

The best teachers teach critical thinking, intellectual humility, and the process of inquiry itself. This requires genuine conceptual understanding of a domain *and* the ability to model another person's mind. Current LLMs simulate this well but don't have the grounded understanding that comes from having a body, experiencing failure, and navigating the world.

## The Hybrid Future

The most likely future isn't AI tutors replacing teachers — it's AI agents augmenting teachers. An AI agent could handle the 70% of instructional time spent on differentiated practice and formative assessment, freeing human teachers to focus on facilitation, mentorship, project-based learning, and social-emotional development.

This requires rethinking teacher training — not just "how to use AI tools" but how to design human-AI collaborative classrooms. The schools getting this right are the ones treating AI as a teaching *partner*, not a replacement staff member.
